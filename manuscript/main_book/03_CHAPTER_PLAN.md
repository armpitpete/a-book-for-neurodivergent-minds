# Chapter 3 Plan

## Chosen chapter

Chapter 3 — You Are Not Broken, But You May Be Overloaded

## Place in book

Chapter 3 completes Part 1.

It sits between:

- Chapter 2 — hidden effort and cost
- Chapter 4 — overload as a specific state

## Chapter job

Chapter 3 should help the reader move from self-blame to a better working explanation.

It should say:

- you are not broken
- your system may be carrying too much
- the problem may be load, mismatch, hidden effort, unclear demands, or repeated strain
- support should reduce load, not shame the reader

## Core point

The problem may not be who you are.

The problem may be what you are carrying.

## What Chapter 3 must do

- reduce shame without pretending everything is fine
- explain load in a broad way before Chapter 4 gives the detailed overload model
- connect hidden effort from Chapter 2 to overload from Chapter 4
- keep language clear for ND and YP readers
- work as a standalone chapter

## Difference from Chapter 2

Chapter 2 asks:

> Why did life feel harder than it looked?

Chapter 3 asks:

> What if the problem is not that I am broken, but that I am carrying too much?

## Difference from Chapter 4

Chapter 3 is broad and reassuring.

Chapter 4 is specific and practical about overload.

Chapter 3 should not teach the full shelf/straw model.

## Suggested structure

1. What this chapter is for
2. If you can only read one thing
3. Start here if
4. Short version
5. What “not broken” means
6. What “carrying too much” means
7. How mismatch creates strain
8. What makes it harder
9. What helps
10. 30 seconds / 3 minutes / 30 minutes
11. Useful scripts
12. Quick summary

## Avoid

- vague reassurance
- false positivity
- saying nothing is wrong anywhere
- blaming the reader
- making the chapter too medical
- repeating Chapter 4 in full
