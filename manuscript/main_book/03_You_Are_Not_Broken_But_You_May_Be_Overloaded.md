# You Are Not Broken, But You May Be Overloaded

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is for the person who has started to wonder:

> Is something wrong with me?

It is also for the person who has been told they are lazy, too sensitive, dramatic, difficult, rude, inconsistent, or not trying hard enough.

This chapter gives a different starting point.

The problem may not be who you are.

The problem may be what you are carrying.

## If you can only read one thing

You are not broken.

You may be carrying too much, too often, with too little support.

That does not mean nothing is wrong.

It means the first useful question may not be:

> What is wrong with me?

A better first question may be:

> What load am I carrying?

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- ordinary life often feels harder than it seems to feel for other people
- you blame yourself before checking the situation
- you can manage for a while and then crash
- people think you are not trying when you are already using a lot of effort
- small demands feel huge when you are already tired
- you feel broken because support has not matched what you need
- you have been trying to cope by hiding the cost
- you need a kinder explanation than personal failure

## Short version

Being overloaded does not mean being broken.

It means the load is too high for the current system, situation, support, and recovery time.

The load may come from:

- sensory input
- unclear demands
- social pressure
- body needs
- change
- masking
- fear of getting it wrong
- doing too much for too long

A better response is not shame.

A better response is to notice the load and reduce what can be reduced.

## What “not broken” means

“Not broken” does not mean everything is easy.

It does not mean you never need help.

It does not mean all problems disappear if people are kind.

It means your difficulty deserves a real explanation, not an insult.

If a phone battery is empty, you do not call the phone lazy.

You charge it.

If a room is too loud, you do not blame your ears for noticing.

You reduce the noise if you can.

If a task has unclear steps, you do not become bad because you need the steps made clearer.

You need better information.

The same principle can apply to people.

Difficulty is information.

It is not automatically proof that you are defective.

## What “carrying too much” means

Carrying too much means your system has more to process than it can safely manage right now.

That load may be visible.

It may also be hidden.

Examples:

- a classroom that is too loud
- a workplace with vague instructions
- a home where people keep interrupting
- a shop with lights, queues, smells, and choices
- an appointment where questions come too fast
- a family event where you have to mask all day
- a school day followed by homework and messages
- a workday followed by travel, chores, and no quiet recovery

Any one of those may be hard.

Several together can become too much.

## How mismatch creates strain

Sometimes the problem is not the person.

Sometimes the problem is the fit between the person and the environment.

A person who thinks best with written steps may struggle when everything is spoken quickly.

A person who needs quiet may struggle in a noisy room.

A person who needs processing time may struggle when people demand instant answers.

A person who can do a task once may not be able to repeat it every day without recovery.

That is mismatch.

Mismatch does not mean the person is worthless.

It means the conditions are not working well enough.

## Why self-blame can feel convincing

Self-blame can feel convincing because it is simple.

It gives one answer:

> I am the problem.

But simple is not always true.

It may ignore:

- how much noise there was
- how unclear the task was
- how much you were masking
- how tired your body was
- how many demands came before this one
- how little recovery time you had
- how much fear you were carrying

Self-blame often arrives faster than understanding.

That does not make it accurate.

## What makes it harder

It gets harder when people:

- praise you only when you hide the cost
- call you difficult when you ask for clearer support
- treat every struggle as attitude
- compare you with people who do not carry the same load
- expect the same capacity every day
- ignore sensory, social, body, or recovery needs
- give vague instructions and then blame you for guessing wrong
- wait until you crash before believing you need help

It also gets harder when you learn to do that to yourself.

You may start judging yourself before you check the load.

## What helps

### 1. Change the first question

Instead of asking:

> Why am I like this?

Ask:

> What is the load?

That question gives you something practical to work with.

### 2. Look for mismatch

Ask:

- Is the room too loud?
- Are the instructions unclear?
- Are there too many choices?
- Am I hungry, tired, in pain, or tense?
- Am I masking too much?
- Do I need written steps?
- Do I need more time?

### 3. Reduce one part of the load

You may not be able to fix everything.

That is fine.

One reduced load can still help.

Examples:

- ask for written instructions
- leave earlier
- reduce noise
- take a break before the crash
- make the task smaller
- ask for one question at a time
- delay one non-urgent thing

### 4. Use support without making it a moral trial

Support is not proof that you are failing.

Support is what helps a person function with less damage.

Glasses are support.

A ramp is support.

A written list is support.

A quiet space is support.

Extra time is support.

Support is not cheating.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> I may not be broken. I may be carrying ____.

Examples:

- too much noise
- too many choices
- too many people
- too much uncertainty
- too much recovery debt
- too much masking

Name one load.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- The thing I blame myself for:
- The load that may be involved:
- One thing that could reduce the load:

Example:

- The thing I blame myself for: not answering quickly
- The load that may be involved: too many questions at once
- One thing that could reduce the load: ask for one question at a time

### 30 minutes

Choose one repeated difficulty.

Map it without shame:

1. What usually happens?
2. What load is present?
3. What support is missing?
4. What smaller version might work?
5. What script could I use?

Example:

- What happens: I avoid forms.
- Load: too many boxes, unclear words, fear of mistakes.
- Missing support: someone to explain the steps.
- Smaller version: fill in one section only.
- Script: “I need help understanding the first part.”

## Useful scripts

### When you are blaming yourself

“I may not be broken. I may be carrying too much.”

### When someone says you are not trying

“I am trying. The load is too high right now.”

### When you need clearer conditions

“I can do more when the steps are clearer.”

### When you need less pressure

“I need the load reduced, not a lecture.”

### When you need support without a long explanation

“I need this made smaller, clearer, or quieter.”

## What not to force

Do not force yourself to prove you are struggling by reaching collapse.

Do not wait until the difficulty is visible before taking it seriously.

Do not call yourself broken when the situation may be too loud, too vague, too fast, too demanding, or too unsupported.

Do not treat support as failure.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need clearer steps
- you need less noise
- you need more time
- you need recovery after effort
- you cannot repeat the same output every day
- you work better when the conditions fit you
- you need support before other people understand why

Needing support does not make you broken.

It means support matters.

## Quick summary

- You are not broken because life has been hard.
- The problem may be load, mismatch, or missing support.
- Ask what you are carrying before blaming who you are.
