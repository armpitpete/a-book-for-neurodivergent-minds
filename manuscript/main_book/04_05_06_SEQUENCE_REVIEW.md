# Chapters 4, 5, and 6 Sequence Review

## Scope

Reviewed the current cleaned drafts as a sequence:

- Chapter 4 — Overload
- Chapter 5 — Shutdown
- Chapter 6 — Meltdown

## Verdict

The sequence works.

The chapters should stay separate.

They form a clear progression:

1. **Overload** — too much load is hitting or being carried by the system.
2. **Shutdown** — the system starts pulling functions down.
3. **Meltdown** — distress may move outward and become visible.

Each chapter still works as a standalone reading unit.

## Chapter 4 job — Overload

Chapter 4 explains cumulative load.

Its core idea is:

> Do not only ask what set me off. Ask what had already filled me up.

It should stay focused on:

- load building up
- the shelf/straw idea
- different kinds of load
- reducing input, demand, and body load
- Tool 8 — Noise Budget

## Chapter 5 job — Shutdown

Chapter 5 explains quiet low-function response.

Its core idea is:

> quiet does not automatically mean fine.

It should stay focused on:

- reduced speech
- reduced movement
- reduced decision-making
- smaller communication
- protecting the recovery window
- Tool 12 — The Quiet Handover

## Chapter 6 job — Meltdown

Chapter 6 explains visible distress and loss of containment.

Its core idea is:

> make the situation smaller and safer.

It should stay focused on:

- less noise
- fewer words
- more space
- safety first
- aftercare before analysis
- avoiding shame and force

## Shared language that is allowed

All three chapters may mention:

- less input
- less demand
- fewer words
- safety
- body needs
- recovery time
- shame not helping

This is allowed because each chapter must survive on its own.

## Main risk

The main risk is repeated explanations of overload, shutdown, and meltdown in all three chapters.

Repeat the short distinction where useful, but do not let each chapter retell the full explanation of the other two.

## Editing rule

Use this simple distinction throughout the book:

- Overload: too much is hitting or filling the system.
- Shutdown: the system pulls functions down.
- Meltdown: distress moves outward or containment is lost.

## Current status

No immediate rewrite needed.

The sequence is sound enough to continue planning the next main-book chapter.
