# Overload

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about overload.

Overload means your system is carrying more than it can safely process, tolerate, or recover from in that moment.

It can happen because of sound, light, people, choices, messages, tasks, pain, hunger, poor sleep, change, uncertainty, masking, or too much effort for too long.

This chapter has one main point:

> Do not only ask what set me off. Ask what had already filled me up.

## If you can only read one thing

Overload is often cumulative.

That means it builds up.

The last thing may not be the whole cause.

The final straw matters, but so does the shelf that was already full.

Ask:

> What has already filled my shelf?

Then reduce one load.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- ordinary sound or light feels too much
- people talking feels physically intrusive
- a small request feels unbearable
- you are losing words, patience, or decisions
- you feel snappy, tearful, panicky, blank, or far away
- you can keep going in public but crash later
- you are blaming yourself for a reaction that may actually be overload
- someone thinks you reacted to one small thing, but you know there was more behind it

## Short version

Overload is not weakness.

Overload is load.

Useful first steps:

1. reduce incoming input
2. reduce demand
3. stabilise the body
4. look at the whole shelf, not just the last straw

Do not try to prove you are fine by pushing harder.

That often makes the bill bigger.

## How overload may feel

Overload can feel sharp or dull.

Sometimes everything is too loud, too bright, too close, too fast, or too complicated.

Sometimes your thoughts snag on ordinary things.

A question, a queue, a light, a message, or a changed plan can feel much bigger than it looks from the outside.

You may feel rude, slow, oversensitive, dramatic, weak, or not adult enough.

Those are common shame-stories.

They are not always true.

A person in overload may be reading the pressure accurately, but have too little spare capacity left to hide it.

## Plain explanation

Overload happens when the total load on your system is too high.

That load may be obvious, but it may also be hidden.

You may be carrying several kinds of load at once:

- sensory load: sound, light, texture, smell, temperature, movement
- social load: talking, being watched, being judged, reading tone or faces
- decision load: too many choices, unclear instructions, changing plans
- emotional load: fear, grief, anger, embarrassment, worry
- body load: hunger, thirst, pain, illness, tiredness, hormones, medication effects
- performance load: masking, pretending, translating, acting fine
- uncertainty load: waiting, not knowing, unclear outcomes

From the outside, people may only see the final straw.

They may think:

> Why are you reacting to that tiny thing?

But that tiny thing may have landed on top of a full shelf.

The real question is not only:

> What happened just now?

It is also:

> What had already filled me up?

That question gives better information.

## Common signs

Possible signs of overload include:

- noise or light suddenly feels painful or invasive
- you cannot filter background information
- simple choices become hard
- you lose words, patience, or flexibility
- you become snappy, tearful, panicky, numb, or blank
- task-switching gets much harder
- clothes, heat, cold, smells, or touch feel worse than usual
- your body feels tight, buzzy, hot, shaky, sick, or exhausted
- you want to escape but cannot explain why
- you function in public and collapse later in private

This list is for recognition, not diagnosis.

## What makes overload worse

Overload often gets worse when you:

- stay in the high-load place because leaving feels awkward
- add more tasks because you feel guilty
- treat warning signs as weakness instead of information
- keep masking when you have no spare capacity left
- switch between too many unfinished tasks
- delay food, water, rest, or medication that helps stability
- argue while already flooded
- keep checking messages or notifications when already frayed
- blame the final straw and ignore the full shelf

A lot of suffering comes from trying to look reasonable while the system is already past its limit.

## What helps

### 1. Reduce incoming input

This may mean:

- leave the room
- step outside
- use headphones or earplugs
- lower brightness
- reduce conversation
- take a quieter route
- stop looking at messages for a while

### 2. Reduce demand

This may mean:

- postpone one task
- ask for one instruction at a time
- choose the easier version on purpose
- stop pretending you are fine
- tell one person: “I am overloaded.”

### 3. Stabilise the body

This may mean:

- water
- food
- warmth
- cooler clothing
- rest
- movement
- pressure
- a quieter position

Body load can make every other load heavier.

### 4. Learn your shelf

Notice what fills you before the visible crash.

Examples:

- poor sleep plus supermarket plus missed lunch plus phone calls
- work meeting plus small talk plus travel uncertainty
- masking all day plus one more request at home
- bright lights plus noise plus decision pressure

When you know your shelf, you can act earlier.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Ask:

> What is the loudest pressure right now?

Then reduce one thing:

- step away
- put on headphones
- drink water
- stop the conversation
- sit down
- lower brightness
- take off the irritating clothing layer

Do one thing only.

### 3 minutes

Sort the load into headings:

- sensory
- social
- task or decision
- body
- emotion
- uncertainty

Put one word beside any active heading.

Example:

- sensory: café
- social: questions
- body: hungry
- uncertainty: waiting

Pick the top two.

Respond to those first.

Do not try to fix all six at once.

### 30 minutes

Build an overload-reduction plan for the next part of the day.

Use these headings:

- input: what can I reduce?
- demand: what can wait?
- body: what basic need needs sorting?
- contact: who needs a short message, if anyone?
- exit: where can I go if this gets worse?

Example:

- input: leave the shop
- demand: do not answer non-urgent messages until tomorrow
- body: eat something plain and drink water
- contact: text “I am overloaded and slower than usual”
- exit: go home earlier

This is not indulgence.

It is load management.

## Useful scripts

### Naming overload

“I’m overloaded. I need to reduce input before I can answer properly.”

### Leaving without a full explanation

“I need to step out now. I’ll come back to this later.”

### Reducing demand

“I can do this more slowly, or I can do less of it today.”

### Asking for a practical change

“Can we turn one thing down? The noise, light, or talking is too much for me right now.”

### Asking for fewer decisions

“Please give me one choice at a time.”

## What not to force

Do not force these when overloaded unless safety requires it:

- crowded shops or complex errands just to get them over with
- conflict-heavy conversations
- long explanations of your whole history
- high-stakes decisions
- social niceness beyond your capacity
- more masking to hide the overload
- staying because leaving would look difficult

Looking fine is not the same as being fine.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need to leave earlier than other people
- you become less articulate under pressure
- a small extra demand lands hard because the shelf was already full
- you need quieter routes, odd timings, special clothes, or recovery time
- you can do something one day and not the next
- you look capable from the outside and still pay heavily afterwards

Hidden effort is still effort.

## Quick summary

- Overload is load, not weakness.
- Do not only blame the final straw; check the shelf.
- Reduce input, demand, or body load before pushing harder.
