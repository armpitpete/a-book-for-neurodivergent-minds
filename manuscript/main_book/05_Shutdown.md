# Shutdown

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about shutdown.

Shutdown is a state where your system starts pulling functions down because it cannot safely keep going at the same level.

It can look quiet from the outside.

That does not mean it feels calm from the inside.

This chapter has one main point:

> quiet does not automatically mean fine.

## If you can only read one thing

Shutdown is not laziness, rudeness, or refusal.

It is often a low-function protective state.

If you may be shutting down, reduce demand first.

Use less speech if speech is getting hard.

Move to a quieter or safer place if you can.

Delay non-urgent decisions.

Do not spend your last energy trying to look normal.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- speaking feels too hard
- you can think more than you can say
- you are going quiet, blank, slow, or flat
- you need to lie down, hide, leave, or stop
- people think you are ignoring them
- people think silence means agreement
- questions make your mind go blank
- you can text a little but cannot talk properly
- you feel far away from your body, words, or surroundings

## Short version

Overload means too much is hitting the system.

Shutdown means the system starts pulling things down.

Meltdown means distress may move outward and become visible.

These states can overlap.

For shutdown, the first useful response is:

1. reduce input
2. reduce demand
3. use smaller communication
4. protect the recovery window

## How shutdown may feel

Shutdown can feel like your system has gone dim.

Not peaceful. Not rested. Dim.

Your thoughts may still exist, but they may feel far away, tangled, or too slow to bring forward.

Words may be present inside your head but refuse to line up at your mouth.

Your body may feel heavy, cold, distant, clumsy, or expensive to move.

You may know what you want to say and still not be able to say it.

You may want people to stop asking questions and also be unable to tell them that.

You may look calm from the outside while feeling partly missing from the inside.

## Plain explanation

Shutdown is often what happens when your system moves from strain into protection.

Instead of going outward, the system may go inward.

Speech may reduce.

Movement may reduce.

Facial expression may reduce.

Decision-making may reduce.

Social access may reduce.

The ability to mask or explain may disappear early.

This can be easy for other people to misread.

They may call it:

- sulking
- being difficult
- ignoring them
- not trying
- attitude
- laziness
- a bad mood

Those labels can be wrong and harmful.

Shutdown is not the same as choosing silence to make a point.

It is not the same as being relaxed.

It is not the same as agreeing.

It is not always the same as wanting to withdraw.

Sometimes the person would like to respond normally and cannot reach that level anymore.

## Common signs

Possible signs of shutdown include:

- speaking much less than usual
- taking longer to answer simple questions
- losing access to words
- giving very short replies
- freezing when asked to choose or explain
- staring, stillness, or slow movement
- reduced facial expression or eye contact
- feeling heavy, foggy, flat, or far away
- wanting to hide, curl up, lie down, or leave
- crying quietly or going numb
- being able to text but not talk
- feeling worse when people keep pressing for answers

This list is for recognition, not diagnosis.

## What makes shutdown worse

Shutdown often gets worse when people:

- keep demanding speech
- ask too many questions
- push for eye contact
- treat silence as consent
- treat silence as disrespect
- touch without checking, unless safety requires it
- keep the person in a loud, bright, crowded, or demanding place
- frame low function as attitude
- force problem-solving before stability
- make the person prove that shutdown is real

A common error is adding moral pressure to a system that already has too little capacity.

That rarely restores function.

It usually burns what little is left.

## What helps

### 1. Reduce demand

This may mean:

- fewer questions
- fewer choices
- less talking
- less need to explain
- less pressure to reassure other people

### 2. Reduce input

This may mean:

- quieter space
- dimmer light
- fewer people
- fewer notifications
- less movement around you

### 3. Use smaller communication

If speech is hard, try:

- one-word answers
- thumbs up or down
- pointing
- texting
- writing
- a saved phone note
- a prepared card

Smaller communication is still communication.

### 4. Stabilise the body

This may mean:

- water
- food
- warmth
- blanket
- pressure
- lying down
- changing uncomfortable clothes
- using a familiar object or routine

### 5. Protect the recovery window

Shutdown may continue after the person looks a little better.

A few words do not always mean full recovery.

Treat the first signs of return as fragile.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Ask:

> Do I need less input, less demand, or a different way to communicate?

Then choose one action:

- stop answering for one minute
- move somewhere quieter
- switch from talking to texting
- sit or lie down
- get water
- show a note
- use one-word replies

### 3 minutes

Build a shutdown-safe response.

Pick one item from each line:

- input: quieter / darker / fewer people
- communication: text / nod / point / one-word replies
- body: water / food / warmth / rest / pressure
- demand: postpone / reduce / stop / leave

Example:

- input: go to the bedroom
- communication: text “I can’t talk properly right now”
- body: drink water and lie down
- demand: cancel the non-urgent call

That is enough for now.

Shutdown is not the moment for a beautiful recovery plan.

It is the moment for reducing damage.

### 30 minutes

Make the next part of the day smaller.

Ask:

- what stops now?
- what can wait?
- what support do I need?
- what communication method is safest?
- what helps me come back?

Example:

- stop: no more phone calls tonight
- wait: email reply and washing up
- support: someone else handles the door
- communication: text only
- recovery: dark room, quiet, water, blanket, no decisions

## Useful scripts

### When you can still say one sentence

“I’m shutting down. I need less input and less talking.”

### When speech is limited

“I can text, not talk.”

### When someone keeps asking questions

“I cannot answer properly right now. Please ask me later.”

### When another person wants to help

“You do not need to explain. I will make this quieter and simpler.”

### After, if someone misread the silence

“I was not ignoring you. My system had gone too far offline to respond properly.”

## Helper note

If someone is shutting down, do not assume silence means yes.

Do not assume silence means no.

Do not assume silence means rudeness.

Use fewer words.

Offer simple choices.

Give time.

Check later, when the person has more access to speech or another way to communicate.

## What not to force

Do not force these during shutdown unless immediate safety requires it:

- long explanations
- emotional processing on demand
- eye contact for reassurance
- big choices
- conflict resolution
- social niceness beyond capacity
- a full return to normal because a few words came back

One small sign of function does not mean shutdown is over.

It may only mean the person managed one expensive signal.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- your speech drops before your understanding does
- you need text, gesture, or silence instead of spoken language
- you look calm while feeling far from okay
- you need to lie down, hide, or leave without a polished explanation
- other people think you are fine because you are quiet
- you need longer to come back than others expect
- you return in fragments rather than all at once

Shutdown is not a character flaw.

It is often the cost of carrying too much for too long.

## Quick summary

- Shutdown is a real low-function state, not laziness or attitude.
- Quiet does not automatically mean fine, consent, refusal, or disrespect.
- Reduce demand, reduce input, use smaller communication, and protect recovery time.
