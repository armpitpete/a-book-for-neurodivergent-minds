# Chapter 7 and Chapters 4 to 6 Sequence Review

## Scope

Reviewed Chapter 7 against:

- Chapter 4 — Overload
- Chapter 5 — Shutdown
- Chapter 6 — Meltdown

## Verdict

The sequence works.

No immediate rewrite is needed.

Chapter 7 should stay separate from Chapters 4 to 6.

## Sequence distinction

Use this distinction throughout the book:

- Chapter 4 — Overload: too much load is hitting or filling the system.
- Chapter 5 — Shutdown: the system pulls functions down.
- Chapter 6 — Meltdown: distress moves outward or containment is lost.
- Chapter 7 — Burnout: depletion builds when strain continues past recovery for too long.

## Chapter 7 job

Chapter 7 is about longer-term depletion.

Its job is:

- explain burnout as more than ordinary tiredness
- connect burnout to repeated overload, masking, demand, and interrupted recovery
- reduce shame around lost capacity
- avoid quick-fix recovery promises
- focus on reduced demand and protected recovery

## Main overlap risk

The main overlap risk is with Chapter 4.

Chapter 4 explains load becoming too much in a shorter-term state.

Chapter 7 explains what may happen when load keeps returning before recovery is complete.

Keep Chapter 4 focused on:

> What is filling the shelf?

Keep Chapter 7 focused on:

> What keeps draining me faster than I can recover?

## Shared language that is allowed

These ideas can appear in several chapters:

- reduce demand
- reduce input
- protect recovery
- avoid shame
- do not push harder when the system is already overloaded or depleted

This is allowed because each chapter must work on its own.

## Editing rule

Chapter 4 = current load.

Chapter 5 = low-function protection.

Chapter 6 = visible distress / safety-first reduction.

Chapter 7 = long-term depletion and recovery debt.

## Current status

No immediate rewrite needed.

Chapter 7 is structurally sound as the burnout chapter.
