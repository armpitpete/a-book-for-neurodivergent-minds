# Sensory Load

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about sensory load.

Sensory load means the amount of input your system has to process from the world and from your body.

That input can include sound, light, smell, texture, touch, movement, temperature, visual clutter, pain, hunger, tiredness, or body signals.

Sensory load is real load.

It is not childish.

It is not fussiness.

It is not weakness.

This chapter has one main point:

> if your system is processing too much input, reducing input is practical, not weak.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by asking:

> Why am I so sensitive?

Start by asking:

> What input is my system trying to process?

Then reduce one input if you can.

That might mean less noise, less light, less smell, less touch, fewer people, a softer item of clothing, a quieter route, or a short break away from the busy place.

You do not have to justify every sensory need before you are allowed to reduce strain.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- noise feels painful, sharp, heavy, or impossible to filter
- light feels too bright, flickery, hot, or invasive
- smells make you feel sick, angry, distracted, or trapped
- clothing, labels, seams, socks, shoes, or textures feel unbearable
- touch feels too much, even if the other person means well
- busy spaces make thinking harder
- you feel worse in supermarkets, classrooms, offices, waiting rooms, buses, trains, or family events
- you hide sensory distress so people do not call you difficult
- you feel ashamed for needing quieter, darker, softer, looser, or less crowded conditions

## Short version

Sensory input uses capacity.

Some people process more input, stronger input, or harder-to-filter input.

If the input is too much, the problem is not automatically your attitude.

The problem may be the load.

Useful first steps:

1. name the strongest input
2. reduce one sensory load
3. stop pretending it is fine if pretending is costing too much
4. protect recovery after high-input situations

## What sensory load means

Sensory load is the work your system does to receive, filter, sort, and tolerate input.

Some input comes from outside you.

Examples:

- sound
- light
- smell
- touch
- texture
- temperature
- movement
- visual clutter
- crowds

Some input comes from inside your body.

Examples:

- hunger
- thirst
- pain
- nausea
- tiredness
- needing the toilet
- heartbeat
- tension
- breath

This matters because a person may look as if they are doing one simple thing, while their system is processing many things at once.

For example, sitting in a classroom may also mean processing lights, chairs, voices, pens clicking, people moving, body heat, clothing texture, fear of being called on, and the effort of staying still.

That is not nothing.

That is load.

## Different kinds of sensory input

### Sound

Sound load can include:

- loud noise
- overlapping voices
- sudden noises
- high-pitched sounds
- background hums
- music you cannot control
- people eating, tapping, sniffing, or clicking

### Light and visual input

Light and visual load can include:

- bright lights
- flicker
- glare
- screens
- fast movement
- cluttered rooms
- busy walls
- too many things to track at once

### Smell and taste

Smell and taste load can include:

- perfume
- cleaning products
- food smells
- smoke
- damp
- body smells
- strong flavours
- mixed smells in shops, buses, schools, or workplaces

### Touch and texture

Touch and texture load can include:

- labels
- seams
- socks
- shoes
- tight clothing
- scratchy fabric
- unexpected touch
- sticky hands
- wet sleeves
- hair touching your face

### Movement and body position

Movement load can include:

- travel
- crowds moving around you
- standing still for too long
- sitting in an uncomfortable chair
- balance demands
- being bumped
- needing to stay still when your body needs movement

### Internal body signals

Body-signal load can include:

- hunger
- thirst
- pain
- tiredness
- illness
- tension
- temperature
- medication effects
- not knowing what your body needs until it is urgent

## How sensory load builds

Sensory load can build quietly.

One sound may be manageable.

One bright light may be manageable.

One scratchy label may be manageable.

But sound plus light plus smell plus touch plus hunger plus social pressure can become too much.

From the outside, someone may only see the final reaction.

They may think:

> Why are you upset about that tiny thing?

But the final thing may not be the whole cause.

It may be the last input on top of many others.

## What makes sensory load worse

Sensory load often gets worse when people:

- tell you to ignore it
- call you too sensitive
- make sensory needs sound childish
- touch you without checking
- force you to stay in high-input places
- make you explain while overloaded
- treat distress as bad behaviour
- expect masking and sensory tolerance at the same time
- only allow support after visible collapse

It can also get worse when you hide the distress for too long.

Masking sensory pain may help you survive the moment, but it can cost you later.

## What helps

### 1. Name the input

Ask:

> What is the strongest input right now?

Examples:

- noise
- light
- smell
- clothing
- touch
- crowding
- hunger
- heat

Naming the input gives you a target.

### 2. Reduce one sensory load

You may not be able to fix the whole environment.

Reduce one thing if you can.

Examples:

- lower brightness
- move away from speakers
- use headphones or earplugs
- change clothing
- step outside
- sit away from the busy area
- open a window if safe and appropriate
- eat or drink if body load is involved

### 3. Stop treating sensory support as failure

Sensory support is practical.

Glasses reduce visual strain.

A coat reduces cold.

Headphones can reduce sound load.

A softer shirt can reduce touch load.

A quieter route can reduce recovery cost.

Support does not mean weakness.

### 4. Plan recovery after high-input places

Some places may be difficult even with support.

Examples:

- supermarkets
- assemblies
- meetings
- public transport
- parties
- hospital or clinic waiting rooms
- open-plan offices
- busy family events

If you know a place is high-input, plan recovery afterward.

Do not fill the recovery gap with another demanding task unless safety requires it.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The strongest input right now is ____.

Examples:

- the light
- the voices
- the smell
- the label
- the heat
- the crowd
- my hunger

Then reduce one input if possible.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- Input that is too much:
- What it is doing to me:
- One reduction I can try:

Example:

- Input that is too much: overlapping voices
- What it is doing to me: I cannot think
- One reduction I can try: step outside for two minutes

### 30 minutes

Map one regular high-input situation.

Use these headings:

1. Place:
2. Main input:
3. Hidden body load:
4. What helps:
5. What recovery is needed after:

Example:

- Place: supermarket
- Main input: lights, noise, choices, people
- Hidden body load: hunger and tiredness
- What helps: list, headphones, quieter time, short visit
- Recovery after: no extra talking for 20 minutes

## Useful scripts

### Naming sensory load

“This is too much input for me right now.”

### Asking for less input

“I need less noise / light / touch / smell / movement.”

### Asking for one change

“Can we reduce one sensory load?”

### Explaining capacity

“This is not fussiness. It is affecting my capacity.”

### Leaving without a long explanation

“I need to step away from the input for a bit.”

## What not to force

Do not force yourself to tolerate every sensory input just to look easy.

Do not treat sensory distress as a moral problem.

Do not wait until you collapse before reducing input.

Do not apologise for every practical adjustment.

Do not let “other people are fine” become proof that you should be fine too.

Different systems process input differently.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need headphones, sunglasses, softer clothes, quieter routes, or breaks
- you cannot ignore input that other people ignore
- your tolerance changes from day to day
- body needs make sensory input harder
- you need recovery after high-input places
- you hide sensory distress and then crash later
- you need support before other people understand why

Sensory load is real load.

Reducing it is practical.

## Quick summary

- Sensory load is real load, not weakness.
- Name the strongest input before blaming yourself.
- Reduce one input where you can, then protect recovery.
