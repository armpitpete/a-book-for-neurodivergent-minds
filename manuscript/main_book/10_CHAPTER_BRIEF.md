# Chapter 10 Brief

## Title

Demand Friction

## Status

Brief v0.1.

## Chapter job

This chapter should explain why a demand can make a task harder, even when the task looks small.

It should reduce shame around starting, switching, choosing, replying, and doing things when pressure is high.

## Core message

A task can look small from the outside and still create a lot of demand inside.

Reducing demand pressure can make action more possible.

## Reader need

The reader may think:

- I am lazy.
- I am avoiding everything.
- I am difficult.
- I should just do it.
- I do not know why being asked makes it harder.
- I can do things when they are my idea, but freeze when they become expected.
- I hate being interrupted or rushed.
- I need the task made smaller, clearer, or less pressured.

This chapter should explain the pattern without shame.

## Must include

- clear opening point
- short version near the top
- plain definition of demand friction
- examples of requests, choices, vague instructions, pressure, timing, and interruption
- distinction between friction and deliberate refusal
- 30 seconds / 3 minutes / 30 minutes actions
- scripts for asking for less pressure or clearer steps
- three-bullet quick summary

## Key distinction

Demand friction is not the same as not caring.

It is the extra resistance created by pressure, uncertainty, interruption, unclear steps, or too many demands at once.

## Suggested scripts

“I am not refusing. The demand pressure is making it harder to start.”

“Please make the first step smaller.”

“I need one instruction at a time.”

“I can do this better if I am not rushed.”
