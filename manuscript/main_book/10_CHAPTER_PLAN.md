# Chapter 10 Plan

## Chosen chapter

Chapter 10 — Demand Friction

## Place in book

Chapter 10 follows:

- Chapter 9 — Sensory Load

It continues Part 2 — What Might Be Happening.

## Chapter job

Chapter 10 should explain demand friction in plain language.

Demand friction means the extra resistance that can appear when a task, request, choice, instruction, expectation, or transition becomes a demand.

It should help the reader understand why “just do it” may not work when the demand itself increases pressure.

## Core point

A task can look small from the outside and still create a lot of demand inside.

The problem may not be refusal.

The problem may be friction.

## What Chapter 10 must do

- explain demand friction clearly
- avoid calling the reader lazy or oppositional
- show how requests, choices, vague instructions, pressure, timing, and interruption can increase friction
- separate demand friction from deliberate refusal
- give small ways to reduce demand pressure
- connect demand friction to overload, shutdown, masking, and burnout without retelling those chapters
- stay clear for ND and YP readers
- work as a standalone chapter

## Difference from Chapter 4

Chapter 4 explains overload broadly.

Chapter 10 focuses on one specific kind of load: demand pressure.

## Difference from Chapter 18

Chapter 18 will be about planning without collapse.

Chapter 10 is not a planning chapter.

It explains why demands can become hard to start, continue, switch into, or complete.

1. What this chapter is for
2. If you can only read one thing

4. Short version

6. What can create friction
7. Why “just do it” may not help

## Avoid

- calling the reader lazy
- framing demand friction as bad attitude
- making it sound like all demands can be avoided
- giving too many productivity tricks
- turning the chapter into a task-management system
- repeating all of Chapter 18 before it exists
