# Demand Friction

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about demand friction.

Demand friction means the extra resistance that can appear when something becomes a demand.

The task may look small from the outside.

Inside, it may feel heavy, blocked, urgent, trapped, confusing, or suddenly impossible to start.

This chapter has one main point:

> the problem may not be refusal. The problem may be friction.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by calling yourself lazy.

Start by asking:

> What is making this demand harder to enter?

The answer might be pressure, unclear steps, too many choices, being watched, being rushed, being interrupted, fear of doing it wrong, or being asked at the wrong time.

Reducing demand pressure can make action more possible.

The first step may need to become smaller, clearer, quieter, slower, or less loaded.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- a task feels harder as soon as someone asks you to do it
- you can do something when it is your idea, but freeze when it becomes expected
- you feel trapped by simple requests
- you avoid messages because replying has become a demand
- choices make you stuck
- vague instructions make you unable to begin
- being watched makes a task harder
- being rushed makes your thinking disappear
- interruptions make you angry, frozen, or lost
- people think you are refusing when you are actually jammed

## Short version

Demand friction is extra resistance around starting, switching, choosing, replying, continuing, or finishing.

It can happen when a task comes with too much pressure.

It can also happen when the task is unclear, badly timed, too big, too watched, or too emotionally loaded.

Useful first steps:

1. name the demand
2. find the friction
3. reduce one pressure
4. make the first step smaller

## What demand friction means

A demand is not only a command.

A demand can be anything that lands as something you now have to do, answer, decide, start, finish, remember, tolerate, or perform.

Examples:

- “Reply to this message.”
- “Choose what you want.”
- “Start your homework.”
- “Fill in this form.”
- “Come downstairs.”
- “Tell me what is wrong.”
- “Get ready quickly.”
- “Just decide.”
- “Do it before you forget.”

Some demands are necessary.

Some are reasonable.

Some are urgent.

But even a reasonable demand can create friction if your system is already under pressure.

Demand friction is the resistance that appears between knowing something needs doing and being able to enter the task.

## What can create friction

### Unclear steps

A task becomes harder when you do not know the first action.

“Clean your room” may contain too many hidden steps.

“Put the clothes in the basket” is easier to enter.

### Too many choices

Choice can become load.

“What do you want?” can feel simple from the outside and huge from the inside.

A smaller choice may help:

> Tea or water?

### Pressure and urgency

Rushing can make the task harder.

The more someone says “just do it now,” the more your system may lock.

### Being watched

Some people lose access to action when someone watches, waits, comments, corrects, or hovers.

The task may become less about doing the thing and more about being observed doing it.

### Interruption

Being pulled out of one task and pushed into another can create friction.

Even a small interruption may break the route your brain was using.

### Emotional load

A task may carry fear, shame, conflict, memory, embarrassment, or past failure.

The visible task may be small.

The emotional demand may be large.

### Too many demands at once

One demand may be possible.

Five demands may jam the system.

The problem may be stacking.

## Why “just do it” may not help

“Just do it” assumes the route into the task is open.

Sometimes it is not.

The person may know the task matters.

They may want it done.

They may understand the consequence.

They may still be stuck at the entry point.

More pressure can make that worse.

It can turn the task into a test of character, obedience, speed, or emotional safety.

A better question is:

> What would make the first step easier to enter?

## Friction is not the same as not caring

Demand friction is not the same as not caring.

It is not automatically deliberate refusal.

It is not automatically laziness.

It is also not a free pass to ignore every responsibility.

It means the path into action needs attention.

The demand may need to be smaller, clearer, slower, less public, less emotionally loaded, or better timed.

## What makes demand friction worse

Demand friction often gets worse when people:

- repeat the same demand louder
- add shame
- watch closely
- rush the answer
- make the task vague
- give several instructions at once
- interrupt without warning
- ask questions while the person is already overloaded
- treat stuckness as bad attitude
- turn every delay into conflict

It can also get worse when you attack yourself for being stuck.

Shame is rarely a good starting tool.

## What helps

### 1. Make the first step smaller

Do not start with the whole task.

Start with the entry point.

Examples:

- open the form
- find the pen
- put shoes by the door
- write one sentence
- clear one plate
- read the first line

### 2. Make the demand clearer

Ask:

- what exactly needs doing?
- what is the first step?
- what can wait?
- what does finished mean?
- how long should this take?

Clear edges reduce friction.

### 3. Reduce pressure

Pressure can block action.

Try:

- fewer words
- more time
- less watching
- one instruction at a time
- a written step
- a lower-stakes version

### 4. Change the entry route

Sometimes the usual route is blocked.

Use another route.

Examples:

- text instead of talk
- timer instead of open-ended task
- music instead of silence
- sit down before starting
- start with the easiest corner
- ask someone to sit nearby without talking

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The demand is ____.

Then ask:

> What is the friction?

Examples:

- too vague
- too many steps
- too watched
- too rushed
- too many choices
- too emotionally loaded

Name one friction point.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- Demand:
- Friction:
- Smaller first step:

Example:

- Demand: reply to the message
- Friction: I do not know how to explain properly
- Smaller first step: send “I’ve seen this and will reply tomorrow”

### 30 minutes

Pick one stuck task.

Map it like this:

1. What is the demand?
2. What makes it harder?
3. What is the smallest first step?
4. What pressure can be removed?
5. What does “good enough” mean here?

Example:

- demand: clean the kitchen
- harder: too many visible jobs
- first step: put rubbish in one bag
- remove pressure: do not finish the whole room
- good enough: sink area clear enough to use

## Useful scripts

### When you are not refusing

“I am not refusing. The demand pressure is making it harder to start.”

### When the first step is too large

“Please make the first step smaller.”

### When instructions are stacked

“I need one instruction at a time.”

### When rushing makes it harder

“I can do this better if I am not rushed.”

### When you need a written step

“Please write the first step down.”

### When someone is hovering

“I can start more easily if I am not watched while I begin.”

## What not to force

Do not force a full productivity system when the problem is one blocked entry point.

Do not call yourself lazy before checking the friction.

Do not turn every task into a moral test.

Do not add more pressure when pressure is the thing blocking action.

Do not assume the whole task must be solved before the first step can happen.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- being asked makes something harder
- vague instructions block you
- you need one step at a time
- choices make you stuck
- pressure makes you slower
- you can start when the task is made smaller
- you need less watching before you can move

Demand friction is real.

Reducing it is practical.

## Quick summary

- A small-looking task can still create a lot of demand inside.
- The problem may be friction, not refusal.
- Make the first step smaller, clearer, slower, or less pressured.
