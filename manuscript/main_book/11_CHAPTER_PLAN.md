# Chapter 11 Plan

## Chosen chapter

Chapter 11 — Transitions, Change, and Being Thrown Off

## Place in book

Chapter 11 follows:

- Chapter 10 — Demand Friction

It continues Part 2 — What Might Be Happening.

## Chapter job

Chapter 11 should explain why changing from one state, place, task, plan, conversation, or expectation to another can be hard.

It should help the reader understand that being thrown off by change is not automatically overreacting.

It may mean the brain had built a route, and the route was suddenly broken.

## Core point

A change can look small from the outside and still break the route your brain was using.

The problem may not be attitude.

The problem may be transition cost.

## What Chapter 11 must do

- explain transition cost clearly
- avoid calling the reader dramatic, rigid, or difficult
- show how changes of task, place, timing, plan, attention, social expectation, and body state can throw someone off
- explain why warning and recovery space can help
- give small ways to reduce transition pressure
- connect transition cost to overload, demand friction, shutdown, and burnout without retelling those chapters
- stay clear for ND and YP readers
- work as a standalone chapter

## Difference from Chapter 10

Chapter 10 explains demand pressure.

Chapter 11 explains transition and change cost.

They overlap when a change also becomes a demand.

## Difference from Chapter 18

Chapter 18 will be about planning without collapse.

Chapter 11 is not a planning chapter.

It explains what happens when a route is interrupted, changed, or replaced.

## Avoid

- calling the reader rigid
- treating change distress as childish
- saying all change can be avoided
- giving too many planning systems
- repeating all of Chapter 10
- taking over the later planning chapter
