# Transitions, Change, and Being Thrown Off

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about transitions and change.

A transition is a move from one thing to another.

It may be a move between tasks, places, plans, conversations, body states, social roles, or expectations.

From the outside, the change may look small.

Inside, it may break the route your brain was using.

This chapter has one main point:

> the problem may not be attitude. The problem may be transition cost.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by calling yourself rigid or dramatic.

Start by asking:

> What route just got broken?

A change can break the route between where your brain was and what it was preparing to do next.

You may need warning, time, a reset, or one change at a time.

That is not childish.

It is practical.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- changes of plan throw you off more than people expect
- interruptions make you lose the task completely
- switching tasks feels painful, sticky, or confusing
- you need warning before leaving, starting, stopping, or changing direction
- you get upset when someone changes a plan at the last minute
- you struggle when people say “never mind, do this instead”
- you cannot restart quickly after being interrupted
- people call you rigid when you feel disoriented
- you need time between social, school, work, travel, home, or rest modes

## Short version

Transition cost is the effort of moving from one state, task, place, plan, or expectation to another.

Some people need more time to switch.

Some people need warning before a change.

Some people lose the whole mental route when a plan changes suddenly.

Useful first steps:

1. name what changed
2. pause before reacting if possible
3. find the new first step
4. reduce extra demands during the switch

## What transition cost means

Transition cost is the effort it takes to move from one thing to another.

It can happen even when the new thing is not bad.

It can happen even when the change is reasonable.

It can happen even when the change is small from the outside.

Examples:

- stopping a game to eat
- leaving the house
- getting out of the car
- switching from school to home
- moving from work to chores
- ending a conversation
- starting a phone call
- changing from quiet time to social time
- going from one task to another before the first task feels complete

The hard part may not be the new thing.

The hard part may be the switch.

## What kinds of change can throw you off

### Task change

This is when you are doing one task and have to move to another.

Example:

You are writing, then someone asks you to tidy, answer a message, or come downstairs.

Your brain may not switch cleanly.

It may feel like being pulled out of one track before the track has finished.

### Place change

This is when you move from one place to another.

Examples:

- bedroom to kitchen
- home to school
- work to bus
- car to shop
- waiting room to appointment room

Each place may have different sounds, lights, people, rules, and expectations.

### Plan change

This is when the expected route changes.

Examples:

- a cancelled appointment
- a different lift home
- a late bus
- a changed meeting time
- someone adding one more stop
- a promised quiet evening becoming social

The distress may come from losing the expected route, not from being stubborn.

### Timing change

This is when something happens earlier, later, faster, or longer than expected.

Even a good thing can become hard if the timing changes suddenly.

### Attention change

This is when your focus is pulled away.

Examples:

- being interrupted mid-sentence
- someone asking a question while you are concentrating
- a notification pulling you out of a task
- noise breaking the thread you were holding

You may need time to find the thread again.

### Social mode change

This is when you have to change how you are with people.

Examples:

- quiet alone mode to polite talking mode
- family mode to work mode
- friend mode to authority-figure mode
- text mode to phone-call mode

That change can cost energy.

### Body-state change

This is when your body state changes or needs attention.

Examples:

- hunger arriving suddenly
- tiredness hitting
- pain increasing
- needing the toilet
- temperature changing
- medication wearing off

A body-state change can throw off the mental route too.

## Why warning helps

Warning gives the brain time to build a new route.

Without warning, the old route may be cut off before the new one exists.

That can feel like being dropped in the middle of nowhere.

Useful warnings can be simple:

- “We leave in ten minutes.”
- “After this, we are going to the shop.”
- “The plan has changed. I will explain the new plan slowly.”
- “You have two more minutes, then we need to stop.”

Warning does not remove every difficulty.

But it can reduce shock.

## What makes transitions worse

Transitions often get worse when people:

- change plans without warning
- rush the switch
- add several changes at once
- talk too much during the change
- treat distress as bad attitude
- expect instant flexibility
- interrupt without giving time to save the task
- ask questions while the person is still reorienting
- say “it does not matter” when it clearly matters to the person

It can also get worse when you blame yourself for needing time.

Needing a reset is not failure.

## What helps

### 1. Name the change

Ask:

> What changed?

Examples:

- the task changed
- the time changed
- the place changed
- the plan changed
- the person changed
- the noise changed
- the expectation changed

Naming the change helps you find the broken route.

### 2. Find the new first step

Do not try to rebuild the whole plan at once.

Ask:

> What is the next small step now?

Examples:

- put shoes on
- save the document
- write down the new time
- stand up
- ask what changed
- take one minute before answering

### 3. Use a reset gap

A reset gap is a small space between one route and the next.

It may be:

- one minute of quiet
- writing the new plan down
- drinking water
- sitting before leaving
- breathing before replying
- putting away the old task before starting the new one

The gap protects the switch.

### 4. Reduce extra demands during the switch

During a hard transition, reduce added pressure.

This may mean:

- fewer words
- one change at a time
- written instructions
- less rushing
- no extra questions yet
- no moral lecture during the switch

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The change is ____.

Examples:

- the plan changed
- I was interrupted
- I have to leave
- the timing changed
- the task changed
- I lost my route

Name the change first.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- Old route:
- What changed:
- New first step:

Example:

- Old route: finish my game, then eat
- What changed: food is ready now
- New first step: pause the game and put my plate on the table

### 30 minutes

Map one regular transition that throws you off.

Use these headings:

1. Transition:
2. What makes it hard:
3. Warning that would help:
4. Reset gap:
5. First step after the switch:

Example:

- Transition: leaving for school or work
- Hard part: too many steps and time pressure
- Warning: ten-minute and two-minute warning
- Reset gap: shoes on, then one minute by the door
- First step: pick up bag

## Useful scripts

### When you need warning

“I need warning before the change.”

### When you need time to switch

“I need a minute to switch.”

### When the route broke

“That change broke the route I was using.”

### When there are too many changes

“Please give me one change at a time.”

### When you need the new plan made clear

“Please tell me the new first step.”

### When someone thinks you are being difficult

“I am not trying to be difficult. I am trying to reorient.”

## What not to force

Do not force instant flexibility when a reset is needed.

Do not call yourself rigid before checking what changed.

Do not let one changed plan become proof that you are bad at life.

Do not add five new demands while you are still trying to switch.

Do not treat warning, time, or written steps as weakness.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need warning before change
- you need time between tasks
- interruptions break your focus
- a changed plan throws you off
- you need the new route written down
- switching social modes takes effort
- you need a reset gap before moving on

Transition cost is real.

Reducing it is practical.

## Quick summary

- A small-looking change can still break the route your brain was using.
- The problem may be transition cost, not attitude.
- Name the change, find the new first step, and protect the switch.
