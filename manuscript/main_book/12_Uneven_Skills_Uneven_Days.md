# Uneven Skills, Uneven Days

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about uneven capacity.

Uneven capacity means your ability to do something can change depending on the day, place, pressure, support, body state, sensory load, recovery, and timing.

You may be able to do something sometimes, but not always.

You may do a hard thing one day and struggle with a simple thing the next day.

That does not automatically mean you are fake, lazy, unreliable, or choosing to be difficult.

This chapter has one main point:

> being able to do something sometimes does not mean you can do it always.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by asking:

> Why am I so inconsistent?

Start by asking:

> What changed between then and now?

Maybe the task changed.

Maybe the place changed.

Maybe your sleep, pain, hunger, sensory load, pressure, recovery, or support changed.

Maybe yesterday used all the energy that today needed.

Capacity can change.

That does not make your difficulty fake.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- people say “but you did it yesterday”
- your skills seem to disappear under pressure
- you can do something at home but not at school, work, or appointments
- you can do a hard task one day and fail at a small task another day
- you are called inconsistent, unreliable, lazy, or dramatic
- you need different support on different days
- you can speak well sometimes and lose words at other times
- you can cope in one place but not another
- you feel ashamed because your capacity changes

## Short version

Ability is not only about knowing how to do something.

It is also about whether you have enough capacity to access that skill right now.

Capacity can be affected by:

- sleep
- food
- pain
- illness
- sensory load
- pressure
- recovery debt
- unclear steps
- change
- social effort
- masking
- fear of getting it wrong

Useful first steps:

1. name the task
2. name what is different today
3. adjust the demand if possible
4. use the smallest workable version

## What uneven capacity means

Uneven capacity means your access to a skill changes.

The skill may still exist.

But access to it may be blocked, reduced, delayed, or made expensive by the conditions around it.

For example:

- you can write clearly at home, but freeze when watched
- you can answer questions in writing, but not out loud
- you can shop in a quiet shop, but not in a crowded supermarket
- you can make food when rested, but not after a high-demand day
- you can travel a familiar route, but not when the route changes
- you can speak politely for an appointment, then lose words afterward

The difference is not always effort.

The difference may be capacity.

## Why “you did it before” may not prove today

“You did it before” can sound logical.

Sometimes it is useful information.

But it is not always proof that you can do it again right now.

A person may manage something once because:

- they had more rest
- the place was quieter
- the steps were clearer
- someone helped
- there was less pressure
- the task was familiar
- they had more recovery afterward
- they masked heavily and paid later

If those conditions change, the result may change too.

The fairer question is not only:

> Have I done this before?

Also ask:

> What conditions made it possible?

## What changes capacity

### Load

More load usually leaves less spare capacity.

A task may be possible in the morning and impossible after a day of noise, people, decisions, messages, and transitions.

### Recovery

If recovery has not caught up, yesterday may still be taking energy from today.

This can make today look worse for no obvious reason.

### Sensory input

Sound, light, smell, touch, movement, temperature, and body signals can change what you can access.

You may know how to do the task and still be unable to do it in that sensory environment.

### Pressure

Pressure can reduce access to speech, planning, memory, and movement.

Being watched, rushed, questioned, or judged can make a known skill harder to use.

### Support

The right support can make a skill accessible.

Examples:

- written steps
- one question at a time
- more time
- quiet space
- a familiar route
- someone explaining the first step

The absence of support can make the same skill disappear.

### Body state

Hunger, thirst, pain, illness, tiredness, hormones, medication effects, or needing the toilet can reduce capacity.

Body load is still load.

### Context

Some skills depend on context.

You may speak easily with one person and freeze with another.

You may read well in quiet but not in a busy room.

You may manage a routine at home but not in an unfamiliar place.

## What makes uneven capacity worse

Uneven capacity often gets worse when people:

- treat yesterday as proof against today
- call changing capacity an excuse
- remove support because you managed once
- expect the same output every day
- ignore recovery cost
- compare you with people under different conditions
- praise masking and then doubt the crash afterward
- make you prove that capacity has changed

It can also get worse when you attack yourself for not matching an old version of you.

The old version may have been carrying a cost you could not keep paying.

## What helps

### 1. Track conditions, not only results

Do not only ask:

> Did I do it?

Ask:

> What made it possible or impossible?

Look at conditions:

- sleep
- food
- noise
- pressure
- support
- timing
- recovery
- unclear steps
- changes before the task

### 2. Use today’s capacity, not yesterday’s shame

Yesterday may give information.

It should not become a weapon.

Ask:

> What is today’s workable version?

### 3. Keep support when it works

Support should not be removed just because it helped.

If written steps, quieter space, or extra time make a task possible, that means the support is useful.

It does not mean the support was never needed.

### 4. Make the task adjustable

Build more than one version:

- full version
- smaller version
- minimum safe version
- postpone version

This avoids all-or-nothing thinking.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> Today is different because ____.

Examples:

- I slept badly
- the room is noisy
- I am being watched
- the plan changed
- I have not recovered
- I do not know the first step
- I am hungry

Name one condition.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- Skill or task:
- What changes my access to it:
- Today’s smallest workable version:

Example:

- Skill or task: reply to email
- What changes access: pressure to explain perfectly
- Today’s smallest version: send “I have received this and will reply properly tomorrow”

### 30 minutes

Choose one skill that feels uneven.

Map it like this:

1. When is it easier?
2. When is it harder?
3. What support helps?
4. What removes access?
5. What is the minimum safe version?

Example:

- Skill: making food
- easier: quiet kitchen, before I am too hungry, simple ingredients
- harder: noise, clutter, no plan, already overloaded
- support: same easy meals, list, clean pan ready
- removes access: too many choices
- minimum safe version: toast, cereal, microwave meal, or asking for help

## Useful scripts

### When someone says you did it before

“I did it before, but the conditions were different.”

### When capacity has changed

“My capacity has changed today.”

### When you can do it sometimes

“I can do this sometimes, but not under every condition.”

### When support made it possible

“The support helped. That does not mean I never needed it.”

### When the task needs adjusting

“I need the task adjusted to today’s capacity.”

### When you need the smallest version

“What is the minimum safe version of this?”

## What not to force

Do not use yesterday as a weapon against today.

Do not remove support just because it worked.

Do not treat inconsistent access as proof of lying.

Do not force the full version when a smaller safe version would protect the day.

Do not make yourself collapse to prove that capacity changed.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- your skills are easier in some places than others
- pressure changes what you can access
- sensory load makes a known task harder
- you need support some days and less support on others
- you can do a task once but not repeatedly
- you need the task adjusted to today’s capacity
- your recovery is slower than people expect

Uneven capacity is real.

Working with it is practical.

## Quick summary

- Being able to do something sometimes does not mean you can do it always.
- Capacity changes with load, context, body state, support, pressure, and recovery.
- Ask what changed before blaming yourself.
