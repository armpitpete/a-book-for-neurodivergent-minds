# Rejection Sensitivity and Fear of Getting It Wrong

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about rejection sensitivity and fear of getting it wrong.

Rejection sensitivity means a possible sign of rejection, criticism, exclusion, disappointment, or disapproval can feel very intense and urgent.

Fear of getting it wrong means a mistake, correction, unclear response, silence, or delay can feel like danger.

The feeling can be real.

The alarm can be strong.

But the conclusion may still need checking.

This chapter has one main point:

> fear is information, but fear is not always evidence.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by deciding:

> They hate me.

Start by asking:

> What do I know, and what am I guessing?

Maybe something really did go wrong.

Maybe the other person is tired, busy, distracted, unclear, or using words badly.

Maybe you made a small mistake, not a life-ending one.

Maybe your alarm is reacting to old pain, past criticism, masking, overload, or too many unclear moments.

The feeling deserves care.

The conclusion still needs checking.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- silence feels like rejection
- a delayed reply feels like danger
- correction feels like being bad
- a small mistake feels huge
- you feel urgent pressure to apologise, explain, fix, or disappear
- you replay messages to find hidden disapproval
- you cannot relax until you know someone is not annoyed
- you feel crushed by criticism even when it is small
- you avoid trying because getting it wrong feels unbearable
- you treat a possible rejection as proof before checking the facts

## Short version

Rejection sensitivity is not childishness.

It is an intense alarm around possible rejection, criticism, exclusion, or disapproval.

Fear of getting it wrong can make small mistakes feel unsafe.

The goal is not to pretend the feeling is fake.

The goal is to pause before acting as if the fear is already proven.

Useful first steps:

1. name the alarm
2. separate feeling from fact
3. identify the guess
4. choose one safe next step

## What rejection sensitivity means

Rejection sensitivity means your system may react strongly to possible signs of rejection.

The sign may be clear.

It may also be uncertain.

Examples:

- someone replies later than usual
- someone uses a shorter message
- a friend seems quieter
- a teacher, parent, manager, or clinician corrects you
- a group talks without you
- someone changes tone
- someone says “we need to talk”
- someone looks tired or annoyed
- someone says no
- someone gives feedback

The alarm may say:

> I am rejected.

But the event may need checking before that becomes the conclusion.

## What fear of getting it wrong means

Fear of getting it wrong means mistakes can feel bigger than they are.

A correction may feel like a verdict.

A small error may feel like danger.

An unclear instruction may feel like a trap.

A form, message, task, appointment, conversation, or test may become harder because the fear of doing it wrong is using so much capacity.

This can lead to:

- overexplaining
- apologising too quickly
- avoiding the task
- asking for reassurance many times
- freezing before starting
- rewriting messages again and again
- hiding mistakes
- feeling unable to try unless you can do it perfectly

Fear of getting it wrong is not proof that you do not care.

It may mean you care so much that the alarm becomes too loud.

## What can trigger the alarm

### Silence

Silence can be hard to read.

It may mean rejection.

It may also mean the person is busy, tired, thinking, distracted, unwell, or unsure what to say.

### Delay

A delayed reply can feel like proof that something is wrong.

Sometimes it is.

Often it is only a delay.

### Correction

Correction can feel like being told you are bad.

But correction may also mean:

- this part needs changing
- this fact was wrong
- this step needs doing differently
- this person is trying to help
- this rule was unclear before

### Tone

Tone can trigger the alarm quickly.

A person may sound annoyed when they are tired.

They may sound sharp when they are rushed.

They may sound flat when they are busy.

Tone matters, but it is not always complete proof.

### Unclear rules

Hidden rules can make mistakes feel more dangerous.

If nobody says the rule clearly, you may feel blamed for not knowing it.

### Past experiences

Old criticism, bullying, exclusion, punishment, or repeated misunderstanding can train the alarm to fire early.

That alarm may be trying to protect you.

It may still need checking.

## Feeling, fact, guess, and next step

When the alarm is loud, separate four things.

### Feeling

This is what you feel.

Examples:

- scared
- rejected
- ashamed
- panicked
- exposed
- stupid
- unsafe

The feeling is real.

### Fact

This is what you know happened.

Examples:

- they have not replied
- they used fewer words
- they corrected one part
- they said no
- they looked away

Facts should be as plain as possible.

### Guess

This is what you think it might mean.

Examples:

- they hate me
- I ruined it
- I am not wanted
- I made them angry
- I will be in trouble

A guess may be true.

It may also be wrong, partial, or too harsh.

### Next step

This is what you can safely do next.

Examples:

- wait before replying
- ask one clear question
- check the facts
- repair one specific thing
- write the message but do not send it yet
- ask a trusted person to help read the situation
- use a tool to separate facts from guesses

The aim is not to kill the feeling.

The aim is to stop the feeling from driving the whole car.

## What makes it worse

Rejection alarm often gets worse when people:

- mock the feeling
- call you dramatic
- give vague answers
- use silence as punishment
- correct harshly
- change tone without explaining
- expect instant calm
- make you guess what you did wrong
- treat one mistake as proof of your whole character

It can also get worse when you respond while the alarm is at its loudest.

That may lead to messages, apologies, arguments, or exits that do not match the actual situation.

## What helps

### 1. Name the alarm

Say to yourself:

> This is a rejection alarm.

Or:

> This is a getting-it-wrong alarm.

Naming it gives you a small gap before action.

### 2. Slow the first response

If it is safe, do not reply from the loudest point of the alarm.

Use a delay.

Examples:

- wait ten minutes
- draft but do not send
- drink water first
- write facts only
- ask for clarification instead of apologising for everything

### 3. Check facts before conclusions

Ask:

- what do I know happened?
- what am I guessing?
- what else could explain this?
- do I need to repair something specific?
- do I need more information?

### 4. Repair small, not huge

If repair is needed, repair the specific thing.

A repair does not need to become a full self-attack.

Example:

> I think my message may have sounded sharper than I meant. I meant ____. Sorry for the confusion.

That is enough.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The alarm is saying ____.

Examples:

- they hate me
- I ruined it
- I am in trouble
- I got it wrong
- I need to fix this now

Then ask:

> Is that fact or guess?

### 3 minutes

Write four lines:

- Feeling:
- Fact:
- Guess:
- Next step:

Example:

- Feeling: rejected
- Fact: they have not replied for two hours
- Guess: they are annoyed with me
- Next step: wait until tomorrow or send one calm check-in if needed

### 30 minutes

Map one rejection alarm.

Use these headings:

1. What triggered the alarm?
2. What did I feel?
3. What facts do I know?
4. What guesses am I making?
5. What else could be true?
6. Is repair needed?
7. What is the smallest safe next step?

Example:

- Trigger: short reply
- Feeling: panic and shame
- Facts: they wrote “OK”
- Guess: they are angry
- Else true: they were busy, tired, or thought OK was enough
- Repair needed: not yet
- Next step: wait, then ask clearly if needed

## Useful scripts

### Naming the alarm

“I am having a strong rejection alarm. I need to check the facts.”

### Asking for clarity

“I may be guessing. Can you clarify?”

### Repairing without panic

“I want to repair this if needed, but I need to understand first.”

### Taking a pause

“I need a pause before I respond.”

### Asking about tone

“I am not sure how to read that. Are you annoyed, or am I misreading it?”

### Keeping repair specific

“I am sorry for that specific thing. I am not going to turn it into proof that I am bad.”

## What not to force

Do not treat the feeling as fake.

Do not treat the feeling as complete proof.

Do not send ten messages from the loudest part of the alarm.

Do not apologise for your whole existence when one specific repair may be enough.

Do not force yourself to keep rereading a message until it hurts.

Do not turn correction into a verdict on your whole worth.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- silence feels scary
- criticism hits hard
- correction makes you freeze
- you need clear words before you can calm down
- you worry about getting things wrong
- you need a pause before responding
- you need help separating facts from guesses

The feeling is real.

The conclusion still needs checking.

## Quick summary

- A rejection alarm can feel real and urgent without being complete proof.
- Separate feeling, fact, guess, and next step before reacting.
- Repair specific things where needed; do not turn one mistake into a verdict on your whole self.
