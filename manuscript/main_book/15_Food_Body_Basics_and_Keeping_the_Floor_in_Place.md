# Food, Body Basics, and Keeping the Floor in Place

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about food and body basics.

Body basics are the ordinary body needs that help keep your capacity from dropping too far.

They include food, drink, sleep, medication, pain, toilet needs, temperature, movement, and sensory comfort.

This is not diet advice.

This is not about weight.

This is not about being good, clean, disciplined, productive, or perfect.

This chapter has one main point:

> body basics are not a reward for coping. They are part of the floor that makes coping possible.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not wait until you have earned food, drink, rest, warmth, pain relief, or the toilet.

Those things are not prizes.

They are part of the floor.

If the floor drops too low, everything else can become harder.

Before calling yourself lazy, dramatic, angry, useless, or broken, ask:

> Has my body floor dropped?

Then check one basic need.

Food.

Drink.

Toilet.

Pain.

Temperature.

Medication.

Rest.

One check is enough to start.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- you forget to eat or drink
- you do not notice body needs until they are urgent
- cooking feels too hard
- you rely on safe foods or easy foods
- hunger changes your mood, speech, patience, or thinking
- tiredness makes small things feel impossible
- pain uses more capacity than other people see
- heat, cold, or clothing discomfort changes how much you can cope
- you delay toilet needs because switching task is hard
- you feel ashamed that basic body care is difficult
- people make food, sleep, hygiene, or medication sound simple when it is not simple for you

## Short version

Your body needs capacity too.

If your body is hungry, thirsty, in pain, too hot, too cold, exhausted, overwhelmed, or uncomfortable, you may have less capacity for everything else.

That does not mean you are weak.

It means the floor is lower.

Useful first steps:

1. check one body basic
2. choose the easiest safe option
3. reduce the next demand if the floor is low
4. do not make perfect care the entry requirement

## What “keeping the floor in place” means

The floor is the basic support underneath the day.

When the floor is steady, tasks may still be hard, but you have more chance of reaching them.

When the floor drops, everything can feel harder.

Examples:

- a message feels impossible because you are hungry
- a conversation goes wrong because you are in pain
- noise feels worse because you slept badly
- a change of plan feels unbearable because you need the toilet
- a small decision feels huge because you have not eaten
- school, work, or appointments feel harder because your medication timing is off
- clothes feel unbearable because you are hot, tense, or overloaded

The task may look like the problem.

But the body floor may also be part of the problem.

## Body basics that affect capacity

### Food

Food gives the body something to work with.

This does not have to mean a perfect meal.

On a hard day, food may mean:

- toast
- cereal
- yoghurt
- soup
- a sandwich
- crackers
- fruit
- cheese
- noodles
- leftovers
- a microwave meal
- a safe food you can actually eat

Easy food counts.

Safe food counts.

Small food counts if that is the only possible start.

### Drink

Thirst can affect thinking, mood, pain, tiredness, and body comfort.

Drink does not need to be complicated.

A glass of water counts.

A drink you can tolerate counts.

A bottle kept nearby can help if switching task is hard.

### Sleep and rest

Sleep changes capacity.

Poor sleep can make sensory input, demand friction, social confusion, and emotional alarms stronger.

Rest also matters, even when sleep is not possible.

Rest may mean lying down, sitting quietly, dimming the room, reducing input, or stopping extra demands for a while.

### Medication and health routines

Some people need medication, inhalers, creams, supplements, medical routines, exercises, or other health tasks.

For anything prescribed or advised by a clinician, follow the instructions you have been given.

The practical point here is not to be perfect.

It is to notice when forgetting or delaying a health routine affects the day.

### Pain and discomfort

Pain uses capacity.

So does nausea, itching, tight clothing, headaches, sore feet, stomach discomfort, cramps, dental pain, or sitting badly for too long.

Pain is not only a side detail.

It can change speech, patience, movement, memory, and tolerance.

### Toilet needs

Toilet needs can become urgent if they are missed or delayed.

Some people do not notice until the signal is strong.

Some people notice but delay because switching task, finding a toilet, asking to leave, or dealing with public toilets is hard.

That delay can use capacity.

### Temperature

Being too hot or too cold can lower capacity.

Temperature can affect mood, pain, sensory tolerance, attention, sleepiness, and irritation.

A jumper, fan, open window, shade, blanket, drink, or clothing change may be a capacity support, not a luxury.

### Movement

Some bodies need movement to regulate.

Some bodies need less movement because they are tired or sore.

Movement support may mean stretching, walking, changing position, sitting differently, rocking, pacing, lying down, or taking a short movement break.

The point is not exercise as achievement.

The point is giving the body what helps it stay more workable.

### Sensory comfort

Sensory comfort is a body basic too.

Clothes, shoes, lights, smells, sounds, textures, posture, chair, room, and touch can all affect the body floor.

A softer shirt, different socks, headphones, lower light, or quieter space may protect capacity.

## Why body needs can be hard to notice

Body needs are not always obvious.

Some people notice them late.

Some people notice them only when they become urgent.

Some people feel “wrong” but cannot tell whether the need is food, drink, rest, pain, toilet, movement, temperature, or sensory comfort.

Some people are focused on a task and do not switch attention to the body.

Some people learned to ignore body signals to survive school, work, family expectations, appointments, or social situations.

This does not mean they do not care.

It may mean the signal is hard to read, late, quiet, confusing, or blocked by demand.

## What makes it worse

Body basics often get harder when people:

- shame safe foods or easy foods
- make cooking the only acceptable success
- treat eating as a moral test
- ignore pain because it is not visible
- expect the same body capacity every day
- rush transitions around meals or toilet needs
- remove rest because tasks are unfinished
- make medication, food, hygiene, or sleep into a character judgement
- give too many options when capacity is already low

It can also get worse when you set the standard too high.

If the only acceptable food is a perfect meal, then eating may become harder.

If the only acceptable rest is perfect sleep, then resting may feel like failure.

If the only acceptable care is a full routine, then a tiny useful step may be ignored.

## What helps

### 1. Check the floor before blaming yourself

Ask:

> Has my body floor dropped?

Check:

- food
- drink
- sleep
- pain
- toilet
- temperature
- medication
- movement
- sensory comfort

You do not have to check all of them every time.

Start with one.

### 2. Use the easiest safe option

The best support is the support you can actually use.

On a low-capacity day, the easiest safe option may be better than the ideal option you cannot start.

Examples:

- cereal instead of cooking
- microwave food instead of no food
- a bottle of water nearby instead of remembering later
- sitting down to brush teeth if standing is too much
- changing one uncomfortable item of clothing
- taking medication with a visible reminder if that is allowed and safe for you

### 3. Keep repeatable basics nearby

Reduce steps where possible.

Examples:

- easy snacks in one place
- a water bottle near the bed or desk
- pain or health items stored where you actually use them
- simple safe foods on a list
- a spare soft clothing item
- a basic “bad day food” shelf if you can have one

This is not laziness.

It is design.

### 4. Lower the next demand when the floor is low

If the body floor is low, the next demand may need to shrink.

Examples:

- send a short reply instead of a full reply
- eat before the form
- drink before the conversation
- use the easiest food before the bigger task
- delay a non-urgent demand until after rest
- ask for the first step instead of the whole plan

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Pick one check:

> Food, drink, toilet, pain, temperature, medication, rest, movement, or sensory comfort?

Then ask:

> Which one is most likely affecting me right now?

Choose one small action if possible.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- My body floor may be low because:
- The easiest safe support is:
- The next demand should be:

Example:

- My body floor may be low because: I have not eaten
- The easiest safe support is: toast
- The next demand should be: one short message, not the whole email

### 30 minutes

Make a bad-day body floor list.

Use these headings:

1. Easiest food I can usually manage:
2. Drink I will actually drink:
3. Pain or comfort support:
4. Temperature support:
5. Sensory comfort support:
6. One task to shrink if the floor is low:

Example:

- Food: cereal, toast, soup, yoghurt, microwave meal
- Drink: water bottle by desk
- Comfort: soft socks, loose top
- Temperature: hoodie or fan
- Sensory: headphones, lower light
- Shrink: send one-line reply instead of full explanation

## Useful scripts

### When you need food first

“I need food before I can think properly.”

### When your capacity has dropped

“I am not being difficult. My body floor has dropped.”

### When perfect food is too hard

“I need the easiest safe food, not the perfect food.”

### When you need help choosing

“Can you help me choose one basic: food, drink, pain, toilet, temperature, or rest?”

### When the next demand must shrink

“I need to deal with the body basic first, then do the smaller version.”

## What not to force

Do not turn food into a moral test.

Do not treat cooking as the only success.

Do not shame safe foods, easy foods, or repeat foods.

Do not wait until you have earned water, rest, warmth, pain care, or the toilet.

Do not make the full routine the only acceptable routine.

Do not ignore medical advice you have been given.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need reminders to eat or drink
- cooking is too hard sometimes
- safe foods keep you going
- easy food is the only possible option
- pain changes your capacity
- toilet needs, temperature, or clothing discomfort affect your mood
- you need support with body basics before you can do the next task

Body basics are not a reward.

They are part of the floor.

## Quick summary

- Food, drink, rest, pain, temperature, toilet needs, medication, movement, and sensory comfort can affect capacity.
- This is capacity support, not diet advice or moral discipline.
- Check one body basic before blaming yourself, then make the next demand smaller if needed.
