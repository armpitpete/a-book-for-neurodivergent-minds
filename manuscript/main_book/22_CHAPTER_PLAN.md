# Chapter 22 Plan

## Chosen chapter

Chapter 22 — Boundaries, Exits, and Reducing Damage

## Place in book

The current working table of contents places Chapter 22 in Part 3 — What Helps in Real Life.

It follows:

- Chapter 21 — Communication Without Guesswork

It comes before:

- Chapter 23 — Repair, Apology, and Not Carrying Everything

## Chapter job

Chapter 22 should explain boundaries, exits, pauses, and damage reduction.

It should help the reader understand that a boundary is not always an argument, punishment, rejection, or attempt to control another person.

It should show how to protect capacity, safety, and dignity when continuing a situation would make harm worse.

## Core point

A boundary is not a failure of care.

Sometimes the safest next step is to pause, leave, reduce contact, or make the situation smaller before more damage happens.

## What Chapter 22 must do

- explain boundaries in plain language
- explain exits and pauses as practical safety tools
- avoid making the reader responsible for saving every situation
- avoid framing boundaries as cruelty, rejection, selfishness, or punishment
- separate boundary, threat, punishment, exit, and repair
- show how boundaries reduce damage in conflict, overwhelm, unsafe demands, sensory overload, and communication breakdown
- include examples for home, school, work, appointments, admin, online spaces, and relationships
- connect boundaries to communication, help-asking, planning, sensory load, burnout, and repair without retelling those chapters
- stay clear for ND and YP readers
- work as a standalone chapter

## Difference from Chapter 21

Chapter 21 explains communication without guesswork.

Chapter 22 explains what to do when clearer communication is not enough, not possible, not safe, or not being respected.

The two overlap when the reader states a clear limit.

## Difference from Chapter 23

Chapter 23 will explain repair, apology, and not carrying everything.

Chapter 22 can mention repair, but should focus on reducing damage first.

Repair can come later if it is safe and needed.

## Avoid

- making boundaries sound aggressive
- making exits sound like failure
- telling the reader to stay in harmful situations to be polite
- making the reader responsible for everyone else’s reaction
- turning every disagreement into a boundary issue
- taking over the repair chapter
- encouraging unsafe confrontation
- too many scripts
