# Work, School, and Being Treated Fairly

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about work, school, and being treated fairly.

This can include school, college, training, volunteering, placements, paid work, unpaid work, apprenticeships, courses, and other structured settings.

These places often have rules, deadlines, people, noise, instructions, expectations, systems, and judgement.

Some of those things may be manageable.

Some may create barriers.

This chapter has one main point:

> being treated fairly is not a special favour.

Support should reduce barriers so the person can do the real task.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by asking:

> Why am I failing when everyone else can cope?

Start by asking:

> What is the actual task, and what barrier is in the way?

Then ask:

> What support would reduce that barrier?

Needing support does not mean you are incapable.

It may mean the task is possible, but the setup is making it harder than it needs to be.

A fair setup does not remove the real task.

It removes unnecessary barriers around the task.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- you feel you are failing at work or school
- you think you should be able to cope like everyone else
- you need support but do not want to be seen as difficult
- the task is possible, but the setup makes it harder
- instructions are unclear
- everything is treated as urgent
- noise, light, movement, or interruptions make work harder
- transitions between tasks or rooms drain you
- deadlines or priority changes overwhelm you
- you need fairness without having to prove you are broken

## Short version

Fairness means the person has a real chance to do the task.

Support is not there to make the task fake.

Support is there to reduce barriers.

Useful first steps:

1. name the real task
2. name the barrier
3. name one practical support
4. ask for the support in clear words if it is safe and useful

The question is not:

> How do I become someone who needs nothing?

The better question is:

> What would make this task possible enough to do?

## What fairness means

Fairness does not always mean everyone gets exactly the same setup.

People may need different supports to have a fair chance at the same real task.

Example:

If a task is to write an answer, the real task is the answer.

The barrier may be unclear instructions, noise, a rushed deadline, or not knowing the expected format.

Support might be:

- written instructions
- an example answer
- a quieter space
- a clear deadline
- one step at a time
- a priority order

Those supports do not make the answer fake.

They make the task more usable.

Fairness means looking at the barrier, not blaming the person first.

## Bad fit, unclear demand, missing support, and unfair treatment

When work or school goes badly, it is easy to call it personal failure.

But there may be different causes.

### Bad fit

A bad fit means the setting, role, task, timetable, sensory environment, or expectations do not match the person well.

Example:

A noisy open room may be a bad fit for someone who needs quiet to process information.

Bad fit does not mean the person has no ability.

It means the setup and the person are clashing.

### Unclear demand

An unclear demand means the task has not been explained clearly enough.

Examples:

- “Sort this out.”
- “Be more proactive.”
- “Do it properly.”
- “Use common sense.”
- “You should know by now.”

These phrases may hide the real expectation.

The person may need clear words, examples, deadlines, and priority order.

### Missing support

Missing support means a practical adjustment or support is absent.

Examples:

- no written instructions
- no quiet space
- no clear priority order
- no break after overload
- no agreed check-in
- no way to ask questions safely
- no support for transitions

Missing support can make a possible task become much harder.

### Unfair treatment

Unfair treatment means the person is treated badly, dismissed, mocked, ignored, punished unfairly, denied reasonable support, or judged by rules that are not applied fairly.

Unfair treatment is not the same as normal challenge.

Challenge can help learning.

Unfairness blocks access, dignity, or safety.

## Practical supports and adjustments

Support should be practical.

It should help with the barrier.

Examples:

### Instructions

Barrier:

> I cannot hold all spoken instructions in my head.

Support:

> written steps, checklist, example, or one instruction at a time.

### Deadlines

Barrier:

> I know the deadline exists, but not the smaller steps before it.

Support:

> deadline plus mini-deadlines, reminder, or first-step check.

### Sensory load

Barrier:

> Noise, light, movement, smell, or interruptions use my capacity before I start.

Support:

> quieter space, headphones if allowed, reduced interruptions, changed seating, or planned breaks.

### Written information

Barrier:

> Spoken information disappears or becomes confused.

Support:

> written summary, email follow-up, printed steps, or permission to take notes.

### Breaks

Barrier:

> I can continue for a while, but then overload builds.

Support:

> short planned breaks before collapse, not only after things go wrong.

### Transitions

Barrier:

> Switching rooms, tasks, people, or rules throws me off.

Support:

> warning before change, clear next step, transition time, or stable routine.

### Priority order

Barrier:

> Everything sounds equally urgent.

Support:

> clear order: first, next, later.

### Workload

Barrier:

> The amount is too large to enter.

Support:

> minimum safe version, staged deadline, reduced duplicate work, or agreed first section.

### Communication

Barrier:

> Hints, tone, and hidden expectations cause confusion.

Support:

> direct words, clear expectations, and specific feedback.

## Asking for fairness without proving failure

You should not have to prove you are broken before asking for support.

A useful support request can focus on the task and the barrier.

Try this shape:

1. The task is ____.
2. The barrier is ____.
3. The support that would help is ____.
4. This would help me do the task by ____.

Example:

- The task is completing the worksheet.
- The barrier is that the steps were spoken quickly and I cannot hold them all.
- The support that would help is written steps.
- This would help me do the task without missing parts.

This keeps the request practical.

It does not require a performance of distress.

It does not require you to list every private detail about your brain or body.

Share only what is needed and safe to share.

## What helps

### 1. Separate task from barrier

Ask:

> What is the real task?

Then ask:

> What is making access to the task harder?

This stops everything becoming “I am failing.”

### 2. Ask for one support first

A long list of support needs may be real.

But one clear support can be easier to ask for and easier for others to act on.

Examples:

- written steps
- priority order
- quieter place
- extra processing time
- one check-in
- clear deadline

### 3. Keep evidence practical

Evidence does not need to be dramatic.

Example:

> When instructions are only spoken, I miss steps. Written steps help me complete the task accurately.

This is practical evidence.

### 4. Notice patterns

If the same barrier appears again and again, write it down.

Examples:

- instructions unclear every week
- deadlines changed without warning
- noisy room before every difficult task
- feedback too vague to use
- priority order missing

Patterns are useful.

They show that the problem is not just one bad day.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The barrier is ____.

Examples:

- unclear instructions
- no priority order
- noise
- spoken-only information
- too many transitions
- deadline too vague
- no safe way to ask questions

Name one barrier.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write four lines:

- Real task:
- Barrier:
- Support needed:
- How it helps:

Example:

- Real task: finish the report
- Barrier: I do not know which part matters first
- Support needed: priority order
- How it helps: I can start with the right section instead of freezing

### 30 minutes

Map one work or school problem.

Use these headings:

1. What is the real task?
2. What is the barrier?
3. Is this bad fit, unclear demand, missing support, or unfair treatment?
4. What support would reduce the barrier?
5. What is safe to share?
6. Who can I ask?
7. What exact words can I use?

Example:

- Real task: attend training
- Barrier: room is noisy and instructions are spoken only
- Type: sensory load plus missing support
- Support: written summary and seat near exit
- Safe to share: I process better with written information and lower noise
- Ask: tutor or manager
- Words: “I can take part better if I have the key steps in writing and can sit near the exit.”

## Useful scripts

### Written instructions

“I can do the task better if the instructions are written down.”

### Priority order

“I need the priority order, not all tasks treated as equally urgent.”

### Naming the barrier

“The task is possible, but the setup is creating a barrier.”

### Asking for support to do the task

“I am asking for support so I can do the work, not avoid it.”

### Asking for a clear deadline

“When does this need to be done, and what is good enough?”

### Asking for reduced sensory load

“I can focus better with fewer interruptions or a quieter place.”

### Asking for a check-in

“Can we check the first step before I do the rest?”

## What not to force

Do not call every access need a personal failure.

Do not treat fairness as a special favour.

Do not disclose more than is safe or needed.

Do not assume all work or school settings will respond well.

Do not make yourself responsible for fixing the whole system.

Do not accept vague criticism as useful feedback if no one can explain what should change.

Do not wait until burnout before asking for practical support if asking earlier is possible.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need written instructions
- you need clearer deadlines
- you need a quieter space
- you need priority order
- you need breaks before collapse
- you need help with transitions
- you need support to do the real task

Being treated fairly is not a special favour.

Support should reduce barriers so the person can do the real task.

## Quick summary

- Fairness means reducing barriers so the real task is possible.
- Separate the task from the barrier before calling it personal failure.
- Ask for practical support that helps you do the work, not avoid it.
