# Systems, Forms, and Official Language

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about systems, forms, portals, paperwork, official wording, and bureaucracy.

These things can appear in work, school, health, money, benefits, bills, housing, courses, appointments, travel, services, and official requests.

They often use language that does not sound like normal speech.

They may ask for evidence, deadlines, reference numbers, accounts, passwords, signatures, uploads, tick boxes, and decisions.

This can make the whole system feel too big to enter.

This chapter has one main point:

> official language often hides the real task.

The first job is to translate the system into one clear next action.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by asking:

> Why am I stupid with forms?

Start by asking:

> What does this system actually want me to do next?

Then find five things if you can:

1. deadline
2. required action
3. evidence needed
4. contact route
5. consequence

You do not have to understand the whole system before taking the next useful step.

You need the next required action.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- forms make you freeze
- official letters make you avoid opening them
- portals, passwords, uploads, or accounts overwhelm you
- you cannot tell what the system actually wants
- you do not know which deadline matters
- you do not know what evidence is needed
- official wording feels threatening even when it may not be
- you miss forms and then feel ashamed
- the whole system feels too large to enter
- you need plain words before you can act

## Short version

Systems can be hard because they often hide simple tasks inside formal wording.

A letter may look huge, but the real task may be:

- reply by a date
- send evidence
- book an appointment
- check a box
- upload a document
- phone or email someone
- wait for the next letter
- do nothing unless something changes

The useful question is:

> What is the next required action?

If there is no action, the useful question is:

> Do I need to keep this safely?

## Why systems can become inaccessible

Systems can become inaccessible for many reasons.

The problem is not always the person.

Sometimes the system is badly designed.

Sometimes the wording is unclear.

Sometimes there are too many steps.

Sometimes the portal times out.

Sometimes the form asks for information you do not have.

Sometimes the same thing has different names in different places.

Sometimes the letter sounds urgent but does not clearly say what to do.

Sometimes the real action is hidden near the end.

Systems can be hard because they may require many skills at once:

- reading dense wording
- understanding official terms
- remembering passwords
- finding documents
- scanning or uploading evidence
- working out deadlines
- making decisions
- tolerating uncertainty
- using a phone call or portal
- staying calm enough to continue

That is a lot.

Avoidance can happen when the system feels too large, too unclear, or too risky.

Avoidance is not the same as laziness.

It may be a sign that the system needs translating into smaller actions.

## Official wording versus real task

Official wording can make a simple task look larger than it is.

Example official wording:

> Failure to provide the requested information by the stated date may affect the processing of your application.

Plain task:

> Send the information they asked for before the date.

Example official wording:

> Please ensure all supporting documentation is uploaded via the online portal.

Plain task:

> Upload the documents they need on the website.

Example official wording:

> You are invited to attend an appointment.

Plain task:

> Check the appointment date and decide whether you can go.

Example official wording:

> No further action is required at this stage.

Plain task:

> Keep the letter. You do not need to do anything now.

The system may use official words.

Your brain may need plain task words.

That is not failure.

That is translation.

## The five things to find

When a system feels too large, look for five things.

You may not find all five.

Find what you can.

### 1. Deadline

The deadline is when something must be done.

Look for words like:

- by
- before
- no later than
- within 7 days
- by the date shown
- appointment date
- closing date

Ask:

> What deadline matters first?

### 2. Required action

The required action is what they want you to do.

Examples:

- complete the form
- sign the page
- send evidence
- upload a document
- attend appointment
- reply to email
- make a payment
- call to rearrange
- check details

Ask:

> What do they actually want me to do?

### 3. Evidence needed

Evidence is what they need you to show, prove, attach, upload, or bring.

Examples:

- ID
- address proof
- payslip
- appointment letter
- medical letter
- bank statement
- form reference
- course details
- receipt

Ask:

> What evidence do they need from me?

### 4. Contact route

The contact route is how you reach the person or system.

Examples:

- phone number
- email address
- portal message
- online account
- postal address
- appointment desk
- named person

Ask:

> How do I contact them if I cannot complete this?

### 5. Consequence

The consequence is what may happen if the action is not done.

Sometimes the consequence is serious.

Sometimes it is minor.

Sometimes it is unclear.

Look for words like:

- may affect
- could delay
- may be cancelled
- may close
- no further action
- reminder
- final notice

Ask:

> What happens if this is not done?

Do not assume every official letter means disaster.

Find the actual consequence if possible.

## Translating forms and letters

### Forms

Forms often ask questions in a way that feels bigger than the answer they need.

Try asking:

> What information is this box asking for?

Not:

> What is the perfect answer that explains my whole life?

If a box asks for a date, give the date.

If it asks for a contact number, give the number.

If it asks for a short explanation, write the smallest clear explanation that answers the question.

### Letters

Letters often include background, rules, warnings, dates, and contact details.

Look for:

- what changed
- what they want
- by when
- what to send
- how to reply
- what happens next

### Portals

Portals can be hard because they mix passwords, screens, uploads, menus, and time limits.

Try to find the next screen, not the whole system.

Ask:

> What is the next button or upload?

### Emails

Official emails may hide the action inside long wording.

Look for the verb.

Examples:

- send
- reply
- book
- confirm
- attach
- upload
- attend
- read
- check

The verb often shows the action.

### Policies

Policies explain rules.

They may not tell you what to do next.

Ask:

> Is this for information, or does it require action?

### Appointments

Appointment information usually needs three checks:

- date and time
- place or link
- what to bring or prepare

### Official instructions

Official instructions may contain several steps.

Write them as a short list if possible.

Example:

1. create account
2. upload ID
3. book appointment
4. wait for confirmation

## What helps

### 1. Make a plain-language task line

Write one sentence:

> This is asking me to ____ by ____.

Example:

> This is asking me to upload proof of address by Friday.

If there is no action, write:

> This is information only. I need to keep it safe.

### 2. Use a document holding place

Have one place for active paperwork.

It can be a folder, envelope, tray, box, note, or digital folder.

The place does not need to be perfect.

It only needs to stop the document disappearing.

### 3. Ask for translation

It is acceptable to ask someone to put the task in plain language.

Examples:

- trusted person
- support worker
- tutor
- manager
- receptionist
- adviser
- helpline
- service contact

Ask for the next action, not the whole system.

### 4. Stop after finding the next action

You may not need to solve everything in one sitting.

Stop when you have found:

- the deadline
- the required action
- the evidence needed
- the contact route
- the consequence

Or stop when you have found enough to take one next step.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> This system is asking me to ____.

If you do not know, write:

> I do not know what this is asking yet.

That is still useful.

It tells you the next task is translation.

### 3 minutes

Write five lines:

- Deadline:
- Required action:
- Evidence needed:
- Contact route:
- Consequence:

Leave any line blank if you cannot find it yet.

Blank means “unknown”, not failure.

### 30 minutes

Choose one form, letter, portal, or official email.

Map it like this:

1. What is this about?
2. Is it information only or action needed?
3. What is the deadline?
4. What is the next required action?
5. What evidence is needed?
6. How do I contact them?
7. What is one safe next step?

Example:

- About: appointment letter
- Information or action: action needed if I cannot attend
- Deadline: appointment date
- Required action: attend or rearrange
- Evidence: bring ID and medication list
- Contact: phone number on letter
- Safe next step: put date in calendar and put letter in bag

## Useful scripts

### Asking for the next action

“What exactly do I need to do next?”

### Asking about deadline

“What deadline matters first?”

### Asking about evidence

“What evidence do you need from me?”

### Asking for plain language

“Can you put the next step in plain language?”

### Asking if action is needed

“Is this for information only, or do I need to do something?”

### Asking for contact route

“Who do I contact if I cannot complete this online?”

### Asking for help entering the system

“Can you help me find the first required action, not the whole system?”

## What not to force

Do not call yourself stupid because official language is hard to read.

Do not try to understand the whole system before finding the next required action.

Do not assume every official letter means disaster.

Do not keep rereading the same page if you need help translating it.

Do not let one missed form become proof that you cannot handle anything.

Do not disclose more private information than the form actually asks for.

Do not keep important paperwork in random places if one simple holding place would help.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- forms make you freeze
- portals overwhelm you
- official wording needs translating
- you need help finding the next action
- you avoid letters because they feel too big
- you need someone to check the deadline
- you need one form box at a time
