# Health, Appointments, and Being Believed

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about health, appointments, and being believed.

Health needs can involve the body, mind, pain, energy, sleep, movement, senses, mood, medication, injury, illness, disability, stress, or daily functioning.

Appointments can involve doctors, nurses, dentists, therapists, hospitals, clinics, reception desks, phone calls, forms, waiting rooms, and follow-up messages.

These situations can be hard when you need to explain what is happening and be taken seriously.

This chapter does not diagnose symptoms or tell you what treatment you need.

It is about preparing, communicating, asking for support, and making the next step clearer.

This chapter has one main point:

> being calm does not mean being fine.

Health needs should be taken seriously before someone has to collapse, perform distress, or prove suffering dramatically.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start by asking:

> Do I look unwell enough to be believed?

Start by asking:

> What is happening, and how is it affecting my life?

Then ask:

> What support or follow-up do I need from this appointment?

A useful appointment note can be short.

Try this shape:

1. What is happening?
2. How long has it been happening?
3. How does it affect daily life?
4. What am I worried about?
5. What do I need next?

You do not have to perform distress for a health need to matter.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- you sound calm, so people think you are fine
- you forget what you needed to say in appointments
- you do not know how to describe symptoms or impact
- you avoid appointments because they are stressful
- you need written information because spoken information disappears
- you need someone with you but feel ashamed
- you were not believed before, so asking again feels hard
- reception calls or booking systems overwhelm you
- you do not know what happens next
- you need help communicating a health need without proving suffering dramatically

## Short version

Health appointments can be hard because they ask you to explain important things under pressure.

You may need to describe symptoms, impact, risk, support needs, and follow-up while tired, worried, overloaded, or rushed.

Useful first steps:

1. write a short appointment note
2. say what is happening and how it affects life
3. ask for the next step in plain language
4. ask for written information or support if needed

The appointment should not depend only on how distressed you look.

It should focus on what is happening, how it affects life, what risk exists, and what support or follow-up is needed.

## Being calm does not mean fine

Some people look calm when they are struggling.

They may have learned to mask pain, fear, confusion, or distress.

They may speak politely because panic would make communication harder.

They may look organised because they brought notes.

They may smile because that is their social habit.

They may answer briefly because they are overloaded.

None of this proves that the health need is minor.

A calm voice can still describe a serious problem.

A quiet person can still need care.

A prepared note can still describe something urgent.

A person should not have to collapse in the room before the need is taken seriously.

## Symptom, impact, risk, support need, follow-up, and belief barrier

It can help to separate six things.

### Symptom

A symptom is what you notice happening.

Examples:

- pain
- dizziness
- low mood
- panic
- fatigue
- numbness
- poor sleep
- nausea
- breathlessness
- sensory overload
- concentration changes

Try to describe the symptom in plain words.

You do not need medical language to begin.

### Impact

Impact is how the symptom affects daily life.

Examples:

- I cannot sleep through the night
- I cannot walk as far as usual
- I am missing school or work
- I cannot concentrate long enough to finish tasks
- I avoid leaving the house
- I need help with meals, washing, or transport
- I am cancelling plans because of this

Impact helps show why the issue matters.

### Risk

Risk is what may happen if the issue is not assessed, supported, or followed up.

Examples:

- symptoms are getting worse
- I may fall
- I may stop eating properly
- I may miss important tasks
- I may not be safe travelling alone
- I may run out of medication
- I may not attend if the appointment is not accessible

Risk does not mean panic.

It means what needs attention.

### Support need

A support need is what would help you access care or manage the appointment.

Examples:

- written information
- slower explanation
- time to process
- someone with me
- quiet waiting area if possible
- reminder text
- clear follow-up plan
- information in plain language
- permission to use notes

Support needs are not special treatment.

They help you use the appointment.

### Follow-up

Follow-up is what should happen after the appointment.

Examples:

- test booked
- referral made
- medication question answered
- safety advice given
- review date agreed
- symptom diary started
- results route explained
- urgent contact route explained

A useful appointment should leave you knowing what happens next.

### Belief barrier

A belief barrier is anything that makes it harder for your need to be understood or taken seriously.

Examples:

- you look calm
- you struggle to explain quickly
- symptoms change day to day
- you mask distress
- previous appointments went badly
- you are afraid of being called anxious
- you cannot remember examples under pressure
- your pain, fatigue, mood, or sensory distress is not visible

A belief barrier does not mean your need is not real.

It means you may need clearer evidence, notes, support, or advocacy.

## Preparing for appointments

Appointment preparation should be simple enough to use.

Do not try to write your whole health history unless that is needed.

Start with a short note.

### Appointment note

Use this:

- Main problem:
- Started when:
- Happens how often:
- Impact on daily life:
- What makes it better or worse:
- What I am worried about:
- What I need today:

Example:

- Main problem: dizziness and fatigue
- Started when: about three weeks ago
- Happens how often: most days, worse in the morning
- Impact on daily life: missing work and avoiding walking far
- Better/worse: worse after standing, better lying down
- Worried about: falling and not managing normal tasks
- Need today: check what might be causing it and what to do next

A note like this helps when words disappear.

It also helps stop the appointment becoming only what you remembered in the moment.

## Asking to be believed and understood

You may not be able to control whether someone believes you.

But you can make the information clearer.

Useful wording can include:

- what is happening
- what has changed
- what you cannot do now
- what you are worried might happen
- what support you need to explain clearly
- what follow-up you need to understand

Examples:

> I may sound calm, but this is affecting my life.

> I need to use my notes so I do not forget important information.

> I am not asking for a diagnosis from myself. I am asking what the next assessment or support step should be.

> Can you explain what I should do if this gets worse?

> Can you write down the next step?

Being clear is not being difficult.

It is trying to make the appointment useful.

## Common appointment examples

### GP or doctor appointment

Useful focus:

- main problem
- how long
- impact
- worry
- what you need next

Possible request:

> I have written the main points because I forget them under pressure.

### Hospital appointment

Useful focus:

- appointment purpose
- symptoms or changes since referral
- questions
- what happens after today

Possible request:

> Can you tell me what happens next and how I will get the results?

### Mental health appointment

Useful focus:

- mood, anxiety, sleep, risk, daily functioning, support
- what has changed
- what helps and what makes things worse

Possible request:

> I may not look distressed, but I am finding daily life harder. I need help explaining the impact.

### Medication question

Useful focus:

- medication name if known
- what question or problem you have
- side effect, missed dose, supply problem, or review need

Possible request:

> Can you explain what I should do next in writing?

### Phone call

Useful focus:

- one sentence reason
- name/date of birth if needed
- what you need from the call

Possible script:

> I need help booking or checking an appointment. I may need you to repeat the details slowly.

### Reception or admin

Useful focus:

- what you are trying to arrange
- what barrier is stopping you
- what contact route or form is needed

Possible request:

> I am not sure which appointment or form I need. Can you tell me the next step?

### Follow-up

Useful focus:

- who contacts whom
- when results are expected
- what to do if symptoms change
- whether another appointment is needed

Possible question:

> If I have not heard anything by that date, who should I contact?

## What helps

### 1. Bring or use notes

Notes are not cheating.

They are support.

They help you remember what matters.

They can also help someone else understand the problem faster.

### 2. Describe impact, not only symptoms

A symptom may be hard to measure.

Impact can make the problem clearer.

Instead of only:

> I am tired.

Add:

> I am so tired that I am missing work and sleeping after basic tasks.

Impact shows what the symptom is doing to life.

### 3. Ask for written next steps

Spoken information can disappear quickly.

Ask:

> Can you write down the next step?

Or:

> Can you send a summary or tell me where this will be recorded?

### 4. Take support if needed

Bringing someone, asking for an advocate, or using notes can be reasonable support.

It does not mean you are incapable.

It means the appointment matters enough to make it accessible.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The health issue I need to explain is ____.

Examples:

- pain
- fatigue
- anxiety
- sleep
- dizziness
- medication question
- appointment follow-up
- symptoms getting worse

Name one issue.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write five lines:

- Main issue:
- Started when:
- Impact on daily life:
- What I am worried about:
- What I need next:

Example:

- Main issue: poor sleep and panic
- Started when: worse over the last month
- Impact: missing work and avoiding messages
- Worried: it is getting worse
- Need next: support options and what to do if it escalates

### 30 minutes

Make one appointment note.

Use these headings:

1. Main problem:
2. When it started:
3. How often it happens:
4. What makes it better or worse:
5. Impact on daily life:
6. What I am worried about:
7. What I need today:
8. What follow-up I need explained:

Keep it short.

The note is there to help the appointment, not to become a perfect report.

## Useful scripts

### Calm but not fine

“I may sound calm, but this is affecting my life.”

### Using notes

“I need to use my notes so I do not forget important information.”

### Written next step

“Can you explain the next step in writing?”

### If it gets worse

“What should I do if this gets worse?”

### Asking for impact to be heard

“The main impact is ____.”

### Asking for follow-up

“When should I expect follow-up, and who do I contact if I do not hear?”

### Asking for slower explanation

“Can you slow that down and say the main point again?”

## What not to force

Do not ignore serious symptoms because you are afraid of being judged.

Do not panic about every symptom as if every appointment is an emergency.

Do not treat calmness as proof that nothing is wrong.

Do not rely only on memory if notes would help.

Do not apologise for needing written information.

Do not make yourself attend alone if safe support is available and useful.

Do not leave without knowing the next step if you can ask.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- appointments are stressful
- you forget words under pressure
- you need notes
- you need someone with you
- you need written information
- you need to ask again
- you were not believed before and now find appointments hard

Being calm does not mean being fine.

Health needs should be taken seriously before someone has to collapse, perform distress, or prove suffering dramatically.

## Quick summary

- Health communication should focus on what is happening, impact, risk, support need, and follow-up.
- Calm presentation does not prove the need is minor.
- Notes, written next steps, slower explanation, and support can make appointments more usable.
