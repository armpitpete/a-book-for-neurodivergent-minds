# Chapter 32 Brief — When the Plan Goes Wrong

## Working title

When the Plan Goes Wrong

## Main job

This chapter helps the reader recover from a plan failing without turning it into shame, panic, self-attack, or giving up completely.

It should teach a small reset process.

The chapter should be practical after a missed task, late start, overload, forgotten message, failed routine, unexpected change, cancelled plan, or collapsed day.

## Core idea

A failed plan is information.

It does not mean the reader is bad, lazy, broken, or hopeless.

The useful question is not “Why did I ruin everything?”

The useful question is “What is the next safe step now?”

## Boundary with Chapter 31

Chapter 31 teaches weekly pacing before the week breaks.

Chapter 32 teaches what to do after the plan has already gone wrong.

It should not repeat the weekly pacing system.

## Reader promise

By the end, the reader should be able to use a short reset process after a plan goes wrong.

## Reader questions

- What actually happened?
- What is still urgent?
- What can be repaired, moved, reduced, or dropped?
- What is the next safe step now?

## Tone rules

- Plain language.
- Young-person-readable.
- ND-safe.
- No shame framing.
- No “just try again” vagueness.
- Avoid too many choices.
- Keep reset steps short.
- Use practical repair language.

## Must include

- Plans fail for real reasons.
- Difference between a plan failing, the day being lost, and the person being a failure.
- Pause before self-attack.
- The reset process: stop, name, sort, choose, repair.
- What is still urgent / what can move / what can be dropped.
- Minimum repair step.
- Scripts for late replies, missed tasks, cancelled plans, needing reset time.
- 30 seconds / 3 minutes / 30 minutes actions.

## Do not include yet

- Full crisis planning.
- Full conflict repair chapter.
- Full productivity system.
- Detailed emotional regulation system.
- Chapter 31’s weekly pacing scan.
