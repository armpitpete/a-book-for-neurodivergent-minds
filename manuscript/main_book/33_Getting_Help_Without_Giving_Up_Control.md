# Getting Help Without Giving Up Control

## What this chapter is for

This chapter is about getting help without feeling that you have lost control.

Many neurodivergent people are told to ask for help.

But “ask for help” can be too vague.

It can also feel unsafe.

Help may have gone wrong before.

Someone may have taken over.

Someone may have judged you.

Someone may have made the task bigger.

Someone may have used help as a way to control you.

This chapter has one main point:

> useful help should make the next step clearer, safer, smaller, or more possible.

Help is not proof that you have failed.

Help should not remove your voice.

Chapter 32 explained what to do after a plan has gone wrong.

Sometimes the next safe step is asking for support.

This chapter is about asking for that support in a specific and bounded way.

## If you can only read one thing

Do not start with:

> I need help with everything.

Start smaller.

Ask:

1. What is the stuck point?
2. What kind of help would make the next step possible?
3. What do I still want to decide myself?

A useful help request is often specific.

Examples:

- “Can you sit with me while I open this letter?”
- “Can you write down what I say?”
- “Can you help me find the first step?”
- “Can you remind me, but not keep asking?”
- “Can you come with me, but let me speak first?”

Specific help is easier to accept.

It is also easier to stop if it becomes unhelpful.

## Start here if

Start here if any of these are true:

- you need help but do not know what to ask for
- people tell you to ask for help but then take over
- you feel ashamed for needing support
- you avoid asking until the problem becomes a crisis
- you need someone to sit with you while you start
- you need someone to write, explain, remind, or make a step smaller
- you want support but not control
- you need help after overload, missed tasks, admin, appointments, school, work, or messages

## Short version

Help is not one thing.

Different stuck points need different help.

Useful first steps:

1. name the stuck point
2. choose one type of help
3. say what you want the person to do
4. say what you still want to decide yourself
5. stop or change the help if it becomes unhelpful

The goal is not to hand over your life.

The goal is to make the next step possible.

## Help is not failure

Needing help does not mean you are lazy, weak, childish, stupid, or broken.

Human beings use help all the time.

Some help is visible.

Some help is hidden.

Examples:

- reminders
- lifts
- forms explained
- notes written down
- calendars shared
- instructions repeated
- someone checking a letter
- someone sitting nearby while a task starts
- someone making a phone call with you

Support is part of how people function.

It is not a moral test.

The question is not:

> Should I need help?

The useful question is:

> What help would reduce damage or make the next step possible?

## Different kinds of help

It helps to name the kind of help you need.

### Practical help

Practical help means someone helps with the physical or task part.

Examples:

- carry something
- drive you somewhere
- post a letter
- help sort papers
- help clean one safe area
- help prepare food

### Thinking help

Thinking help means someone helps sort information or choices.

Examples:

- list the options
- find the deadline
- break the task into steps
- help decide what is urgent
- help compare two choices

### Communication help

Communication help means someone helps with words, messages, forms, calls, or appointments.

Examples:

- write down what you say
- help draft a reply
- sit with you during a call
- help explain the problem
- ask for written instructions
- help remember what was said

### Body-doubling

Body-doubling means someone is present while you do the task.

They do not have to do the task for you.

Their presence helps you start, continue, or stay with it.

Examples:

- sit nearby while you open letters
- stay on a call while you tidy one area
- work quietly in the same room
- check in at the start and end of a task

### Emotional steadiness

Emotional steadiness means someone helps reduce panic, shame, or overwhelm.

Examples:

- speak calmly
- remind you of the next step
- not rush you
- not make the problem bigger
- stay with you while you calm down

### Advocacy

Advocacy means someone helps you be heard or treated properly.

Examples:

- come to an appointment
- help explain access needs
- ask for a reasonable adjustment
- help challenge unclear instructions
- help make sure the next steps are written down

### Protection

Protection means help that keeps you safer.

Examples:

- helping you leave an unsafe situation
- helping you contact a safe person or service
- helping you avoid someone who is pressuring you
- helping you get urgent support if safety, health, housing, or money is at risk

Protection is not weakness.

It is safety.

## Good help and unhelpful control

Good help makes the next step clearer, safer, smaller, or more possible.

Good help may sound like:

- “What do you want me to do?”
- “Do you want advice, company, or practical help?”
- “Do you want me to write, or do you want to write?”
- “Do you want me to come with you but stay quiet?”
- “Do you want to stop?”

Unhelpful control may sound like:

- “Give it to me. I’ll do it all.”
- “You always mess this up.”
- “Just do what I say.”
- “You’re making a fuss.”
- “I know what is best.”
- “You asked for help, so now I decide.”

Help should not make you smaller.

Help should not remove your choice when choice is still possible.

There may be emergencies where other people need to act quickly to keep someone safe.

But ordinary support should still respect your voice as much as possible.

## Asking for one bounded thing

A bounded request is a help request with edges.

It says what you need and what you do not need.

Examples:

- “Can you sit with me for ten minutes while I start this form?”
- “Can you help me find the deadline, but not do the whole thing?”
- “Can you read this letter and tell me the main point?”
- “Can you remind me once at 6pm?”
- “Can you come to the appointment and help me remember, but let me answer first?”

Bounded help can feel safer because it is not endless.

It also gives the other person clearer instructions.

## Keeping control where possible

Keeping control does not mean doing everything alone.

It means keeping choice where choice is possible.

Useful questions:

- What do I want help with?
- What do I not want help with?
- What do I want to decide myself?
- What can the other person do if I freeze?
- What should they not do unless I ask?

Examples:

- “Please write down my words, but do not change them without asking.”
- “Please sit with me, but do not read the letter unless I ask.”
- “Please remind me once. If I say stop, stop.”
- “Please help me ask for written instructions, but let me speak first.”

These are not rude.

They are instructions for safer support.

## When you do not want advice

Sometimes you need help, but not advice.

Advice can be too much when you are already overloaded.

You can ask for a different kind of help.

Examples:

- “I do not need advice yet. I need help calming the task down.”
- “Please do not problem-solve yet. Can you just sit with me while I start?”
- “I need you to write down what I say, not decide for me.”
- “I need one next step, not a full plan.”

Advice is not always bad.

But it should match the moment.

If the problem is overload, more ideas may not help.

## When help is not safe

Not every person is safe to ask.

Some people use help to judge, shame, control, gossip, punish, or take over.

If someone regularly makes things worse, they may not be the right person for that kind of help.

You may need a different person, a smaller request, written support, a professional, an advocate, or a safer route.

If there is risk of harm, abuse, neglect, exploitation, or serious danger, the issue may need protection, not only ordinary help.

You deserve support that does not make you less safe.

## What helps

### 1. Ask for the action, not the feeling

“I need help” can be hard to answer.

Try asking for the action.

Examples:

- “Can you read this and tell me the deadline?”
- “Can you sit nearby while I make the call?”
- “Can you write down the three steps?”
- “Can you help me choose which task is urgent?”

Action requests are clearer.

### 2. Ask for one next step

When the problem is large, ask for one next step.

Examples:

- “What is the first thing to do?”
- “Can you help me find the first sentence?”
- “Can you help me make this smaller?”
- “Can you help me decide what can wait?”

One step is often enough to restart movement.

### 3. Use a stop signal

A stop signal tells the other person when help is becoming too much.

Examples:

- “Pause.”
- “Too much.”
- “I need quiet.”
- “Please stop giving options.”
- “I need to decide.”

A stop signal is not rejection.

It is a way to keep help usable.

### 4. Thank without over-apologising

You can thank someone without treating your need as a crime.

Examples:

- “Thank you. That helped.”
- “Thanks for sitting with me.”
- “That made the first step easier.”
- “I appreciate the help.”

You do not have to keep saying sorry for needing support.

### 5. Notice what kind of help worked

Afterwards, notice one useful thing.

Examples:

- body-doubling helped me start
- written steps helped more than verbal advice
- one reminder helped, repeated reminders did not
- having someone at the appointment helped me remember
- practical help helped more than emotional advice

This can make future requests clearer.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Finish this sentence:

> The help I need is ____.

Examples:

- someone to sit with me
- someone to write down my words
- someone to help find the deadline
- someone to remind me once
- someone to come with me
- someone to make the first step smaller

Name one kind of help.

That is enough.

### 3 minutes

Write three lines:

- Stuck point:
- Help I want:
- What I still want to decide:

Example:

- Stuck point: I cannot open the letter
- Help I want: sit with me while I open it and help find the deadline
- What I still want to decide: what reply I send

### 30 minutes

Make a small support request map.

Use these headings:

1. What am I stuck on?
2. What kind of help would make the next step possible?
3. Who might be safe enough to ask?
4. What exactly do I want them to do?
5. What do I not want them to do?
6. What do I still want to decide myself?

Keep it short.

This is not a full support plan.

It is one bounded request.

## Useful scripts

### Asking for practical help

“Can you help me do this one practical step?”

### Asking for thinking help

“Can you help me work out what the first step is?”

### Asking for writing help

“Can you write down what I say without changing the meaning?”

### Asking for body-doubling

“Can you sit with me for ten minutes while I start?”

### Asking for advocacy

“Can you come with me and help me ask for the next steps in writing?”

### Asking without advice

“I do not need advice yet. I need help making the next step smaller.”

### Keeping control

“Please help with this part, but let me decide the final answer.”

### Stopping unhelpful help

“Pause. This is becoming too much. I need to slow it down.”

## What not to force

Do not treat help as proof that you are failing.

Do not ask an unsafe person for sensitive help if there is a safer route.

Do not let someone take over everything just because you asked for one thing.

Do not keep accepting help that makes the problem bigger.

Do not apologise so much that the actual request disappears.

Do not ask for advice when what you need is presence, writing, quiet, or one small step.

Do not expect yourself to do everything alone just because help has gone wrong before.

## You are not failing if

You are not failing if:

- you need someone to sit with you
- you need help finding the first step
- you need written words
- you need someone at an appointment
- you need a reminder
- you need help after overload
- you need to say “no advice yet”
- you need to stop help that is becoming too much

Help is not the opposite of independence.

Useful help can be part of staying in control.

## Quick summary

- Help does not have to mean being taken over.
- Useful help makes the next step clearer, safer, smaller, or more possible.
- Ask for one bounded kind of help and name what you still want to decide yourself.
