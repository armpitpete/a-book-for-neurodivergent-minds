# Tool 4 — The Capacity Check

## What this tool is for

This tool helps you check your current capacity before you blame yourself.

Capacity means what you can realistically access right now.

It can change from day to day, hour to hour, and setting to setting.

The aim is not to prove you can do everything.

The aim is to match the task to the capacity you actually have today.

## Use this when

Use this when:

- you could do something before but cannot do it now
- your skills feel uneven
- you are calling yourself lazy, weak, dramatic, or useless
- you are pushing through when your body or brain is already overloaded
- a task that is usually possible has become too hard
- you need to choose a smaller version of the task
- you need to protect tomorrow instead of draining everything today

## Default line

> What capacity do I actually have right now?

## 30 seconds

Pick the closest line:

- I can do the full task.
- I can do a smaller version.
- I can do the first step only.
- I can prepare for the task but not do it.
- I can ask for help.
- I need to stop and recover first.

Then choose the matching action.

Do not choose the action for your best day.

Choose the action for today.

## 3 minutes

Use this shape:

1. The task is: ____.
2. My current capacity is: high / medium / low / very low.
3. A realistic version is: ____.
4. The safe stop point is: ____.

Example:

1. The task is: clean the kitchen.
2. My current capacity is: low.
3. A realistic version is: clear the sink only.
4. The safe stop point is: sink cleared or ten minutes done.

## 30 minutes

Use this table if the day has several tasks.

| Task | Capacity needed | Capacity today | Smaller version |
|---|---|---|---|
| ____ | high / medium / low | high / medium / low / very low | ____ |

Then mark each task:

- do now
- shrink
- delay
- ask for help
- drop for today

Do not turn this into a productivity test.

The point is to stop pretending every day has the same access.

## Script or template

Use one of these:

- “I do not have full capacity for this today.”
- “I can do a smaller version.”
- “I can start, but I need a stop point.”
- “I need help with the first step.”
- “This is a low-capacity day, so I am reducing the task.”
- “Being able to do it sometimes does not mean I can do it always.”

## Stop point

Stop when the task matches current capacity.

Do not raise the demand just because you managed one step.

Do not use a low-capacity day as proof that you have failed.

The tool has worked if the task, support, or stop point is more realistic than before.
