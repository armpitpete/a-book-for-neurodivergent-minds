# Overload

## What this chapter is about

This chapter is about overload: the state where your system is taking in, carrying, or processing more than it can handle safely.

It is likely to help if you often seem fine until suddenly you are not, or if you have spent years being told that you are overreacting when what was really happening was accumulation.

The point of this chapter is to help you:

- recognise overload earlier
- stop confusing overload with moral weakness
- see what kind of load is hitting you
- respond before the bill gets worse

## If you need the short version first

Overload is often cumulative.

It is not always dramatic.

The thing that tips you may not be the thing that filled you.

If you are already near the edge, do this first:

- reduce one incoming pressure
- sort the load into categories later
- leave earlier than your shame wants

## How this may feel

Overload can feel sharp or dull.

Sometimes it feels like everything is too loud, too close, too bright, too fast, too much.

Sometimes it feels like your thoughts are snagging on ordinary things: a question, a light, a clothing label, a decision that should take ten seconds and now feels like being asked to lift furniture with your teeth.

Sometimes it feels like anger with nowhere to go. Sometimes like tears that make no sense. Sometimes like going flat, quiet, foggy, or unreachable.

You may feel rude, slow, stupid, oversensitive, dramatic, weak, or not coping like an adult.

Those are common interpretations.

They are not always accurate.

A person in overload is not necessarily misreading reality. They may be reading the pressure very accurately while lacking the spare capacity to disguise it.

## Plain explanation

Overload is what happens when the total load on your system exceeds what you can process, tolerate, or recover from in the moment.

The load may come from one source, but more often it is cumulative.

You may be carrying several kinds of load at once:

- sensory load
- social load
- decision load
- emotional load
- uncertainty load
- body load such as hunger, pain, lack of sleep, temperature, illness, or hormonal shifts
- performance load from masking, suppressing, translating, or appearing fine

This matters because overload is often misunderstood as an isolated reaction to one small thing.

From the outside, it can look as if a bright light, a question, a queue, a child making noise, or a changed plan caused the problem.

Often that thing was simply the last object on an already full shelf.

Other people may only see the final straw.

They do not see the shelf.

That is one reason neurodivergent people are so often told they are overreacting.

Overload is also not always dramatic.

It can look like:

- going quiet
- becoming irritable
- losing words
- struggling to transition
- freezing over simple tasks
- needing to leave suddenly
- getting disproportionately upset by interruption
- making more mistakes
- becoming physically clumsy or tense
- needing solitude with unusual urgency

That makes it easy to miss, especially if you are used to measuring distress by whether it looks visible enough to other people.

A useful shift is this:

Do not ask only, “What set me off?”

Also ask, “What had already filled me up?”

That question gives you more truthful data.

## Common signs

Possible signs of overload include:

- noise or light that used to be tolerable suddenly feels unbearable
- people talking to you feels physically intrusive
- you cannot filter background information properly
- you lose access to language, patience, or decision-making
- small changes feel bigger than they should
- you become snappy, tearful, panicky, or blank
- starting or switching tasks gets much harder
- your body feels tight, buzzy, hot, shaky, nauseous, or exhausted
- touch, clothes, heat, cold, smells, or textures become much harder to tolerate
- you want to get away immediately, even if you cannot explain why
- you keep functioning in public and then collapse later in private

Again, this is for recognition, not diagnosis.

## What often makes it worse

Overload usually gets worse when you:

- stay in the same high-load environment because leaving feels awkward
- keep adding tasks because you feel guilty about slowing down
- treat your early warning signs as weakness instead of data
- force more social performance than your system can afford
- keep switching between unfinished tasks
- postpone food, water, rest, movement, or medication that affects stability
- argue with other people while your system is already flooded
- keep checking messages, news, or notifications when already frayed
- blame the last straw and ignore the full pile

## What often helps

What helps depends on the type of load, but several principles are widely useful.

### 1. Reduce the incoming load

This may mean:

- leaving or shortening the exposure
- using headphones, earplugs, sunglasses, layers, or quieter routes
- stepping out of the group
- reducing conversation, screens, or choices

### 2. Reduce the hidden load

This may mean:

- stopping the performance of “I’m fine”
- telling one person you are overloaded instead of acting normal at full cost
- dropping one non-essential task
- choosing the easier version on purpose

### 3. Stabilise the body

This may mean:

- water
- food
- cooler or warmer clothing
- different posture
- movement, stillness, pressure, or rest depending on what your body needs

### 4. Learn your shelf, not just your straw

Track what tends to fill you up before the visible crash.

For example:

- bad sleep plus supermarket plus delayed meal plus phone calls plus bright weather
- work meeting plus small talk plus travel uncertainty plus scratchy clothes
- masking all day plus one request at home

When you know the shelf, you can intervene earlier.

## What to try now

### 30 seconds

Do one fast overload check.

Ask:

- what is the loudest pressure right now?
- what is the easiest pressure to reduce right now?
- do I need less input, less demand, or more bodily stability first?

Then do one thing only:

- step outside
- put on headphones
- drink water
- stop the conversation
- sit down
- take off the offending clothing layer
- lower the brightness

### 3 minutes

Sort the load into categories.

Write or think through these headings:

- sensory
- social
- task or decision
- body
- emotional

Then circle the top one or two.

Example:

- sensory: loud café, bright light
- social: too many people asking questions
- body: hungry, headache

Now respond to the top two, not all five.

Many overloaded people make the recovery attempt too ambitious.

They try to repair everything at once and end up proving only that they are still overloaded.

### 30 minutes

Build an overload-reduction plan for the next part of the day.

Choose one action under each heading:

- **input**: what can I reduce?
- **demand**: what can wait?
- **body**: what basic need needs sorting?
- **contact**: who needs a short message, if anyone?
- **escape**: where can I go if this gets worse?

Example:

- input: leave the shop and stop the music in my headphones
- demand: do not answer non-urgent messages until tomorrow
- body: eat something plain and drink water
- contact: text “I’m overloaded and slower than usual”
- escape: go home by taxi instead of forcing the bus

This is not indulgence.

It is load management.

## Useful scripts

### Script 1 — naming overload simply

“I’m overloaded. I need to reduce input before I can answer properly.”

### Script 2 — leaving without a full essay

“I need to step out now. I’ll come back to this later.”

### Script 3 — reducing demands

“I can do this more slowly, or I can do less of it today.”

### Script 4 — asking for a practical change

“Can we turn one thing down? The noise, light, or talking is too much for me right now.”

These are useful because overload often steals language first.

You do not need a beautifully defended argument.

You need a sentence that still works when your system does not.

## What not to force

Do not force these when overloaded unless safety requires it:

- crowded shops, long queues, or complex errands just to get them over with
- conflict-heavy conversations
- long explanations of your whole history
- high-stakes decisions
- social niceness beyond your actual capacity
- more masking to hide the overload from people who have not earned the performance
- continued exposure because you do not want to look difficult

A lot of unnecessary suffering comes from trying to look reasonable to people who are not paying the cost.

## You are not failing if…

You are not failing if:

- you need to leave earlier than other people
- you become less articulate under pressure
- a small extra demand lands like an insult because the shelf was already full
- you need quieter routes, odd timings, special clothes, repeated routines, or recovery days
- you can do something one day and not the next
- you look capable from the outside and still pay heavily for it afterwards

Variation is not moral weakness.

Capacity is not fixed.

And hidden effort is still effort even when no one claps for it.

## Quick summary

Overload is cumulative, not always dramatic, and not the same thing as weakness.

Do not only blame the last straw.
Study the shelf.
Reduce incoming load.
Stabilise the body.
Use short scripts.
Leave earlier than your shame wants.
